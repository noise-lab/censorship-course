# Lecture 8 — Speaker Notes (Net Neutrality)

Per-slide context + clickable links. In-deck notes (the `::: {.notes}` blocks) are also visible via reveal.js speaker view (press **S**). Every URL was verified this session or is a canonical org landing page.

| Slide | Context + Links |
|---|---|
| **The Network Should Not Pick Winners** | Open with the principle, not the law. Net neutrality is the policy expression of the **end-to-end argument** — Saltzer, Reed, Clark, ["End-to-End Arguments in System Design" (1984)](https://web.mit.edu/Saltzer/www/publications/endtoend/endtoend.pdf). The network is a dumb pipe; intelligence lives at the edges. **Tim Wu coined "network neutrality"** in ["Network Neutrality, Broadband Discrimination" (2003)](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=388863) — the canonical academic origin. Book §4.3. Whole lecture is about why such a simple idea produced 15+ years of regulatory whiplash. |
| **Why This Belongs in a Censorship Course** | Tie back to Roberts (Fear / Friction / Flooding). Throttling is textbook friction — you don't have to ban a site, you just make it slow enough that most people give up. Net neutrality rules are one of the few legal instruments that directly constrain that friction. Same levers regulators police for *competition* are levers for **speech**. |
| **The Lineage: From Common Carriage to End-to-End** | Net neutrality inherits two older traditions. **Common carriage (law):** obligations centuries old (innkeepers, ferries), formalized for telecom in the **1934 Communications Act, Title II** — the phone company can't refuse to connect your call because it dislikes who you're calling. **End-to-end (engineering):** Saltzer, Reed & Clark, ["End-to-End Arguments in System Design" (1984)](https://web.mit.edu/Saltzer/www/publications/endtoend/endtoend.pdf) — keep the network dumb and general-purpose; a dumb pipe *can't* pick winners by design.<br><br>Reclassifying broadband as Title II in 2015 was an attempt to map the old common-carrier duty onto the modern dumb pipe. The fight over classification is really a fight over whether broadband is "the phone network" (**carriage**) or "an information service" (**content**) — the exact hinge the [2025 Sixth Circuit ruling](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/ca6/24-3449/24-3449-2025-01-02.html) turned on. |
| **How Networks Interconnect** | Two basic money relationships: customer-provider (one AS pays another) and settlement-free peering (barter). Book §4.3 Internet business model figure. Preferences are implemented with BGP local-preference manipulation. Foreshadow the trap: a congested *peering link* looks identical to *throttling* from the user's seat, but one is an economics dispute and the other is information control. |
| **The 2015 Open Internet Order** | [FCC 2015 Open Internet Order (GN Docket No. 14-28)](https://www.fcc.gov/document/fcc-releases-open-internet-order). Reclassified broadband ISPs as **Title II common carriers**. Bright-line rules: no blocking, no throttling, no paid prioritization, plus a no-unreasonable-interference/disadvantage catchall and transparency requirements. Title II is the hook everything hangs on — it's the statutory authority that lets the FCC impose the bright-line rules. The rules don't ban *all* prioritization — an ISP may prioritize all video over all bulk transfer. What's barred is *picking among competitors*: FaceTime over Skype, or Netflix over a rival. Held up in [*USTA v. FCC*, D.C. Cir. 2016](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Telecom_Association_v._Federal_Communications_Commission). |
| **A Microscope on ISPs — and a Blind Spot** | The 2015 order trained a microscope on **ISPs** but left CDNs, clouds, search, and social platforms *outside* the FCC's remit. Book §4.3's "barking up the wrong tree" point. Discrimination, prioritization, and blocking remained fair game above the ISP layer — where the most acute modern threats (deplatforming, ranking, app-store removal) sit. Foreshadow Ch. 3: even if NN rules were airtight, they'd miss where information control actually concentrates today. **The 2024–2026 app-store cases are the sharpest illustration:** on **Apr 23, 2025** the European Commission issued its first-ever DMA fines — [**€500M against Apple**](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/news/commission-finds-apple-and-meta-breach-digital-markets-act) for anti-steering (Art. 5(4)) and €200M against Meta — with a 60-day cease-and-desist. [Apple's appeal was filed 7 July 2025](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/07/07/apple-appeal-eu-fine-app-store.html) and is pending before the EU General Court. On the US side, [**DOJ v. Apple** (D.N.J. 2:24-cv-04055)](https://www.justice.gov/archives/opa/pr/justice-department-sues-apple-monopolizing-smartphone-markets) survived Apple's motion to dismiss on [**30 June 2025**](https://www.mintz.com/insights-center/viewpoints/2025-07-02-judge-allows-justice-departments-iphone-monopolization-suit) — still in active pretrial discovery in 2026. Point: the *actual* gatekeepers now are Apple and Google, not Comcast. |
| **Repeal, Reinstate, Repeat** | The whiplash IS the story. Table timeline: **2015** [Open Internet Order](https://www.fcc.gov/document/fcc-releases-open-internet-order) → **2017** [Restoring Internet Freedom Order](https://www.fcc.gov/document/fcc-releases-restoring-internet-freedom-order) (Ajit Pai FCC) → **2024** [Safeguarding and Securing the Open Internet](https://www.fcc.gov/document/fcc-restores-net-neutrality) (Jessica Rosenworcel FCC) → **2025** vacated. Summary: [Net neutrality in the United States (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_neutrality_in_the_United_States). A rule that can be reversed every four years is a weak foundation for a *right* — partly why advocates moved to legislation and to the states. |
| **The Rules Are Struck Down (2025)** | **Marquee current-event case:** *Ohio Telecom Association v. FCC*, 150 F.4th 694 (6th Cir., Jan 2, 2025) — vacated the FCC's 2024 net-neutrality order. Read the opinion: [Justia case page](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/ca6/24-3449/24-3449-2025-01-02.html) · [CRS explainer LSB11264 — "No More Deference"](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/LSB11264) · [Wikipedia summary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ohio_Telecom_Association_v._FCC) · [Global Freedom of Expression case page](https://globalfreedomofexpression.columbia.edu/cases/ohio-telecom-association-v-federal-communications-commission/). The key doctrinal driver was [*Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo*, 603 U.S. 369 (2024)](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/23pdf/22-451_7m58.pdf) — which overruled **Chevron deference** and required courts to exercise independent judgment on statutory interpretation. The 6th Circuit quote to land in class: *"Applying* Loper Bright *means we can end the FCC's vacillations."* Two teaching points: (1) Post-*Chevron*, the regulatory ping-pong may finally stop — in the *deregulatory* direction. (2) The same ruling weakens federal *preemption* of states, so California and Washington's mirror laws keep ISPs largely in line nationwide anyway.<br><br>**The aftermath — states fill the vacuum:** with broadband back under Title I federally, [AEI — After Net Neutrality: The Return of the States (Jan 2025)](https://www.aei.org/technology-and-innovation/after-net-neutrality-the-return-of-the-states/) argues the ruling reopens the door for state rules and risks "balkanizing" the Internet — beyond California, note New York's **Affordable Broadband Act** (rate regulation) and Maine's broadband-privacy law. A user in Los Angeles now has stronger enforceable protections than one in Dallas or Phoenix. |
| **Elsewhere, the Rules Are Statute, Not Ping-Pong** | **Fresh on the sender-pays front:** the **European Commission rejected "fair share" / network-usage fees on Big Tech** (Jul 31, 2025), with a spokesman saying "imposing a network fee is not a viable solution" — [Reuters — Network fee on Big Tech not a viable solution to boost EU digital rollout, EU says (Jul 2025)](https://www.investing.com/news/stock-market-news/network-fee-on-big-tech-not-a-viable-solution-to-boost-eu-digital-rollout-eu-says-4163135). This tracks the July 28, 2025 US–EU trade fact sheet in which the EU confirmed it would not adopt network usage fees; the file now moves into the **Digital Networks Act** (proposed Nov 2025). Telcos (Deutsche Telekom, Orange, Telefónica, Telecom Italia) had pushed for content providers to pay; a coalition of 80+ groups including EFF opposed it as a net-neutrality threat.<br><br>The comparative point for class: the EU bound openness to a **statute** — **Regulation (EU) 2015/2120** — so the baseline is stable and the fights are at the margins (specialized services, zero-rating), whereas the US bound it to an FCC *classification* that flips each administration. After the 2021 CJEU rulings against Telekom Deutschland/Vodafone, **BEREC's June 2022 guidelines** treat application-specific zero-rating as a violation. **India** went further and earlier: **TRAI's Feb 2016** differential-pricing ban outlawed content-based pricing outright, ending **Free Basics** there. Zero-rating is friction-by-pricing — a *tax on access* to non-favored content — see the zero-rating deck (Ch. 4). |
| **"Was Comcast Blocking Netflix?" (2014)** | Popular story said ISPs were **blocking** Netflix; even [John Oliver ran with it on *Last Week Tonight* (Jun 1, 2014)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_Neutrality_(Last_Week_Tonight_with_John_Oliver)) — that segment famously flooded the FCC comment system, driving ~45,000 comments in a week and helping push the FCC toward Title II reclassification in 2015. Reality was more complicated — congestion can occur at *many* points along the path: ISP↔transit interconnections, inside a transit provider's network, transit↔content-provider links. Book's centerpiece for teaching the difference between a *speech* problem and an *economics* problem. |
| **The Evidence: Congestion in Transit, Not the ISP** | M-Lab tests *appeared* to show ISP congestion — but the bottleneck was inside a transit provider (**Cogent**), and the cross-AS test path made it hard to localize. The subtlety the book stresses: the M-Lab test traversed multiple ASes end-to-end, so it measured the *worst* hop, not the ISP. And Cogent was itself *prioritizing* retail over wholesale traffic during congestion — behavior easy to mistake for ISP throttling. See [Nick Feamster's CITP blog analysis (Mar 2015)](https://blog.citp.princeton.edu/2015/03/25/why-your-netflix-traffic-is-slow-and-why-the-open-internet-order-wont-necessarily-make-it-faster/) for the measurement subtleties. |
| **Two Sides of a Classic Peering Dispute** | Netflix and Comcast reached a direct paid-interconnection deal in **Feb 2014**; Netflix reduced traffic through Cogent, congestion cleared. See [Quartz's inside story](https://qz.com/256586/the-inside-story-of-how-netflix-came-to-pay-comcast-for-internet-traffic) and [Larry Downes in *Forbes*](https://www.forbes.com/sites/larrydownes/2014/11/25/how-netflix-poisoned-the-net-neutrality-debate/). Tim Wu at the time called it *"the water in the basement for the Internet industry"* — the first "paid on-ramp" arrangement. Crux distinction: a *peering / interconnection dispute* is about **economics** (who pays to upgrade a link), not about **blocking, throttling, or paid prioritization** in the speech sense. Related, but distinct. **The modern analog is carriage disputes between streamers and pay-TV distributors, not ISPs and CDNs.** The [**Disney–Charter Spectrum blackout**, Aug 31–Sep 11, 2023](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disney%E2%80%93Charter_Communications_dispute) — 10 days dark on ESPN/ABC — settled with a "transformative" deal that folded Disney+ and ESPN+ into the cable bundle ([Deadline](https://deadline.com/2023/09/disney-spectrum-reach-carriage-agreement-ending-10-day-impasse-espn-monday-night-football-1235542828/)). The sequel: [**Disney–YouTube TV blackout** began 10:59 pm ET Oct 30, 2025](https://variety.com/2025/tv/news/youtube-tv-disney-blackout-deal-renewal-espn-abc-1236567658/), lasted **17 days**, and ended with ESPN Unlimited folded into YouTube TV by end of 2026. Same shape as Comcast-Netflix — economic bargaining over who pays whom — but now streaming-native. |
| **Why the 2014 Disputes Faded** | Book's market-incentives argument. Today an access ISP has every reason to provision links well to Netflix, Amazon, Google, YouTube — customers depend on them; much ISP infrastructure runs on those clouds; an ISP whose customers can't reach big sites has no business. Push students: is good behavior driven by incentives *durable*, or just contingent? Where could it break? (Last-mile monopoly, vertically integrated ISP-owned content.) |
| **The Many Faces of "Net Neutrality"** | Disentangle the bundle. Speech-relevant bright-line rules (blocking, throttling, paid prioritization) vs. the public-utility regulatory apparatus (Title II classification, USF fees, outage reporting, privacy) vs. peering/interconnection economics. For *this* course, only the bright-line rules sit squarely in the information-control frame. |
| **Where the Threat Actually Lives Now** | Bright-line rules target the **ISP**, but leverage to block access to swaths of content has moved **up the stack** to a few cloud hosts, CDNs, search engines, app stores, and social platforms. One decision removes content for millions — Ch. 3's consolidation story. **Live examples:** (a) the Nov 18, 2025 Cloudflare outage knocked X, ChatGPT, and much of the top-1M offline — [Cloudflare post-mortem](https://blog.cloudflare.com/18-november-2025-outage/); (b) **Apple pulled ~25 VPN apps from the Russian App Store on July 4, 2024** at Roskomnadzor's demand ([TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2024/07/08/apple-removes-vpn-apps-at-request-of-russian-authorities-say-app-makers/), [Moscow Times](https://www.themoscowtimes.com/2024/07/04/russia-forces-apple-to-remove-vpns-from-app-store-a85608)) — by year-end **~60 more had vanished**; (c) [India's Ministry of Home Affairs ordered Apple + Google to pull Cloudflare's 1.1.1.1, Hide.me, and PrivadoVPN in late Oct 2024](https://www.techradar.com/vpn/vpn-services/vpns-are-disappearing-from-indias-app-stores-and-a-2022-law-may-be-the-culprit) — first major enforcement of India's 2022 CERT-In VPN logging rule. Net neutrality polices the *pipe*; **modern information control sits at the *app-store console*.** One phone call from a government to Apple's compliance team removes an entire category of privacy tools — no BGP hijack, no DPI, no ISP required. |
| **Summary** | The principle outlives the rules: *"the network should not pick winners among applications"* is a durable analytical frame even when the formal regulation is not in force. **Related recent developments to have handy for Q&A:** BEREC opened a **2026 public consultation on 5G network-slicing guidance** (approved for consultation at the [67th BEREC ordinary meetings Jun 2-3, 2026](https://www.berec.europa.eu/en/public-consultations-calls-for-inputs/public-consultation-on-the-draft-berec-further-guidance-on-5g-network-slicing?language_content_entity=en)), with feedback due **6 August 2026** — the substantive question is whether 5G slices can be sold as differentiated QoS without crossing into paid-prioritization discrimination. The [BEREC Open Internet page](https://www.berec.europa.eu/en/all-topics/5g?language_content_entity=en) tracks the file. **5G slicing already commercial in the US:** T-Mobile launched [**T-Priority** for first responders](https://urgentcomm.com/public-safety/t-mobile-formally-launches-t-priority-for-first-responders-powered-by-5g-network-slicing-technology) (formally rolled out 2025; $7.50/mo add-on on the Essentials First Responder plan) and [**SuperMobile** for business customers](https://www.rcrwireless.com/20250827/5g/t-mo-5g-network-slice) (Aug 27, 2025) — the first nationwide commercial slice. Verizon Frontline Network Slice went live in ~30 markets in mid-2025. AT&T's *Turbo* is a $7/mo speed-prioritization add-on but not slice-based. **State-level US persistence:** *ACA Connects v. Bonta* (9th Cir. Jan 28, 2022) upheld [California's SB 822 (2018)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California_Internet_Consumer_Protection_and_Net_Neutrality_Act_of_2018) — see [CRS product LSB10693](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/LSB10693). ISPs ultimately abandoned the challenge — [CA DOJ — California's Net Neutrality Law Is Here to Stay (May 2022)](https://oag.ca.gov/news/press-releases/attorney-general-bonta-california%E2%80%99s-net-neutrality-law-here-stay). Washington, Oregon, Vermont, Colorado, Maine, New Jersey have some form of state NN law. Next deck: mechanics of paid prioritization and zero rating specifically. |
| **Content-Provider Peering, 2024–2026 Edition** | New row for the peering-dispute arc. The 2014 Comcast-Netflix template — big content pays big pipe — is *not* what dominates 2024-26 disputes. Instead, control has migrated to **carriage between streamers and pay-TV distributors** (economic bargaining that looks like the old peering fights but happens above the transport layer). Anchors: [**Disney vs Charter Spectrum**, Aug 31–Sep 11, 2023](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disney%E2%80%93Charter_Communications_dispute) — 10-day ESPN/ABC blackout resolved by folding Disney+/ESPN+ into the cable bundle; and [**Disney vs YouTube TV**, Oct 30 – Nov 14, 2025](https://variety.com/2025/tv/news/youtube-tv-disney-blackout-deal-renewal-espn-abc-1236567658/) — 17 days dark, ~10M YouTube TV subscribers affected, ended with ESPN Unlimited bundled in by end-2026 ([Fortune wrap-up](https://fortune.com/2025/11/15/disney-youtube-blackout-ends-espn-streaming-carriage/), [ESPN release](https://www.espn.com/espn/story/_/id/46969585/disney-reaches-new-deal-youtube-tv-ending-blackout)). YouTube TV also fought Paramount, Fox, and NBCU renewals in 2025 without a blackout, and dropped Univision at end-Sep 2025. Streaming-native peering: [**Netflix ad tier + password-crackdown**](https://www.antenna.live/insights/a-first-look-at-the-impact-of-netflixs-password-sharing-crackdown) added ~50M subs (238M → 301M) by end-2024; [Amazon Prime Video pushed 85% of ~130M US subs onto ads by default on Jan 29, 2024](https://www.emarketer.com/content/amazon-prime-videos-2024-ad-launch-turned-streaming-on-its-head) — the biggest overnight shift in streaming economics since Netflix launched. **YouTube Content ID scale (2025):** [YouTube processed **2.5 billion** Content ID claims in 2025](https://torrentfreak.com/youtube-processed-2-5-billion-content-id-copyright-claims-in-2025/) (up 14% YoY); cumulative rightsholder payouts >$12B lifetime. |
