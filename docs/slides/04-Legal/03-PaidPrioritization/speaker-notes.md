| Slide | Context + Links |
|---|---|
| **Control by Pricing, Not Blocking** | Economics half of Ch. 4. Prior deck covered the *policy fight*; here the focus is *mechanisms* — fast lanes, sponsored data, zero rating — and the central claim from the book: pricing is a *soft* form of information control. Book §4.3–4.4. |
| **Pricing as a Tax on Information** | Anchor everything in the "tax on access" framing from Ch. 1. Book is explicit: pricing introduces Roberts's **friction** — no one is *blocked*, but the price steers. Paid prioritization restricts who receives what information based solely on ability or willingness to pay. Cite Roberts, [*Censored* (Princeton, 2018)](https://press.princeton.edu/books/hardcover/9780691178868/censored). |
| **Two Mechanisms, One Effect** | Keep the distinction crisp: **paid prioritization** changes *speed* (discrimination inside the network); **zero rating** changes *price* (discrimination on the bill). Both pick winners among applications — exactly what net neutrality forbids. The [FCC 2015 Open Internet Order](https://www.fcc.gov/document/fcc-releases-open-internet-order) banned paid prioritization outright but only reviewed zero rating case-by-case. |
| **A Quick Detour: The Internet's Business Model** | Customer-provider vs. settlement-free peering. Paid prioritization and peering disputes both live here and are easy to confuse. Net-neutrality rules govern the *access* ISP's treatment of traffic; peering disputes are economic negotiations *between* networks. Same plumbing, different question. |
| **Was Comcast "Blocking" Netflix?** | 2014: streaming congestion spikes; popular story said ISPs were blocking. Reality: classic peering dispute — more economics than censorship. [Quartz on the inside story](https://qz.com/256586/the-inside-story-of-how-netflix-came-to-pay-comcast-for-internet-traffic) · [Larry Downes in *Forbes*, "How Netflix Poisoned the Net Neutrality Debate"](https://www.forbes.com/sites/larrydownes/2014/11/25/how-netflix-poisoned-the-net-neutrality-debate/) · [Nick Feamster on why NN rules wouldn't fix it](https://blog.citp.princeton.edu/2015/03/25/why-your-netflix-traffic-is-slow-and-why-the-open-internet-order-wont-necessarily-make-it-faster/). M-Lab tests *looked* like ISP throttling but the congestion was in Cogent, and the end-to-end test crossed multiple ASes. Distinguishing blocking/throttling/paid prioritization from an economic peering dispute is the analytical skill here. |
| **The Rules: Net Neutrality's "Bright Lines"** | 2015 FCC Open Internet Order — Title II common carrier classification. Bright-line rules: no blocking, no throttling, no paid prioritization. Carve-out for "reasonable network management" (security, congestion). Rule does *not* ban all prioritization: an ISP can always prioritize all video over all bulk transfer. Line is about *application-specific* discrimination (FaceTime over Skype, Netflix over a rival). |
| **The Regulatory Pendulum** | Table timeline: **2015** Open Internet Order → **2017** [Restoring Internet Freedom Order](https://www.fcc.gov/document/fcc-releases-restoring-internet-freedom-order) → **2024** [Safeguarding order](https://www.fcc.gov/document/fcc-restores-net-neutrality) → **2025** struck down. See [Net neutrality in the United States (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_neutrality_in_the_United_States). Underlying *principle* — the network shouldn't pick winners — outlives the formal rules. |
| **A 2025 Vignette: US Net Neutrality Falls in Court** | *Ohio Telecom Association v. FCC*, 150 F.4th 694 (6th Cir., Jan 2, 2025) — read the opinion at [Justia](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/ca6/24-3449/24-3449-2025-01-02.html) · [CRS explainer LSB11264](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/LSB11264) · [Wikipedia summary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ohio_Telecom_Association_v._FCC). First major telecom ruling after [*Loper Bright v. Raimondo* (2024)](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/23pdf/22-451_7m58.pdf) ended *Chevron* deference — court read the statute itself instead of deferring to the FCC. Public-interest groups declined to seek Supreme Court review, so this is settled for now. Mechanism matters as much as outcome: post-*Loper Bright*, agency rules built on "ambiguous" statutes are far more fragile. |
| **Why the Sky Didn't Fall** | Book's nuance: don't oversell the doom. Market forces, not regulation, keep US ISPs mostly honest *for now*. 2014-style peering disputes largely dead. But that's exactly why the threat has moved — read the next slide. |
| **The Threat Moved Up the Stack** | Book's key reframing: acute threats no longer at the ISP layer. Shifted to consolidated platforms — cloud hosts, CDNs, app stores, search engines, social media, streaming. **Live examples:** (a) Nov 18, 2025 [Cloudflare outage](https://blog.cloudflare.com/18-november-2025-outage/) took X, ChatGPT, much of the top-1M offline for hours — no attack, just internal misconfig; (b) the **DMA app-store fines** ([€500M Apple + €200M Meta on Apr 23, 2025](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/news/commission-finds-apple-and-meta-breach-digital-markets-act)) and Epic Games' twin US wins — [Epic v. Google 9th Cir. affirmed July 31, 2025](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2025/07/31/24-6256.pdf), [Epic v. Apple contempt Apr 30, 2025](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/05/07/apple-says-epic-games-contempt-ruling-could-cost-substantial-sums-.html) — all target *app-store* rules, not carrier rules. Where NN 2015 targeted Comcast, DMA 2024 targets Apple and Google. Ties to Ch. 3. |
| **Paid Prioritization's Next Form: 5G Slicing** | **Fresh regulatory front — lead with this:** at its 67th meeting (**June 2–3, 2026**) BEREC approved a **draft Further Guidance on 5G Network Slicing** — an annex to its Open Internet Regulation guidelines — for public consultation (open to **Aug 6, 2026**). It gives operators a list of generic slicing situations to self-assess against net-neutrality rules, drawing the line between compliant *specialised services* and prohibited discrimination: [BEREC — Public consultation on draft Further Guidance on 5G Network Slicing (June 2026)](https://www.berec.europa.eu/en/public-consultations-calls-for-inputs/public-consultation-on-the-draft-berec-further-guidance-on-5g-network-slicing?language_content_entity=en). The regulator is drawing the wireless fast-lane boundary *now*, before content-provider slices exist.<br><br>Fast lanes moved into the radio: slicing carves one network into logical lanes with guaranteed performance. Shipped deployments (no speculation): **T-Mobile T-Priority** — full commercial launch **Feb 20, 2025**, billed as the first commercially available 5G network slice; first responders get up to **5×** an average user's resources during congestion (T-Mobile Newsroom / UrgentComm, descriptive cite). **AT&T "Turbo"** (2024): **$7/month** to prioritize a subscriber's traffic (Fierce Network, descriptive cite). Today's slices serve public safety / enterprise; the open net-neutrality question is whether a **content-provider** slice (a paid "premium lane" for a chosen app) follows — exactly the application-specific discrimination the bright-line rules targeted, and with no US rules in force nothing formally forbids it. |
| **Zero Rating** *(section divider)* | Pivot to §4.4. Zero rating is the cleanest illustration of "control by pricing": nothing is blocked, but some content is free and the rest costs money — and in markets with high data costs, that difference is decisive. |
| **What Is Zero Rating?** | ISP charges by data used but *doesn't meter* traffic to certain services. Common in developing regions with expensive cellular data; often video streaming. US example: **T-Mobile's Binge On** (Nov 2015) — streaming video from ~40 partners exempt from data cap, capped at 480p. See [Stanford Cyberlaw: "T-Mobile's Binge On Violates Key Net Neutrality Principles"](https://cyberlaw.stanford.edu/publications/t-mobiles-binge-violates-key-net-neutrality-principles/) and [Northeastern's Wehe/Meddle technical analysis](https://wehe.meddle.mobi/bingeon.html) — the latter caught T-Mobile throttling *all* video traffic, not just partners, and identified a "free-riding" bug letting any traffic ride the zero-rated lane. Purely economic mechanism — no DPI required to "block" anything. Book §4.4: zero rating "does not forbid users from reaching content, but it makes some content meaningfully cheaper to use and therefore more likely to be used." **The 2024–2026 US analog is streaming-bundle wireless plans.** T-Mobile's **Experience More** plan ($85/mo) includes Netflix and Apple TV for free; **Experience Beyond** ($100/mo) adds Hulu — Hulu ad-tier folded in [Jan 24, 2024](https://deadline.com/2024/01/hulu-t-mobile-streaming-bundle-apple-tv-netflix-1235694383/). That's ~$400/yr of "free" streaming attached to a wireless plan. Is that zero rating? The data still counts against the cap; the *content subscription* is bundled — arguably a stronger version of the mechanism (the app is not just cheap to run, it's free to *have*). Notable friction: [T-Mobile ended free Apple TV Jan 1, 2026](https://www.howtogeek.com/t-mobile-is-removing-this-free-perk-from-some-plans/) after Apple raised the wholesale price. |
| **Facebook's "Free Basics"** | Download an app; get free access to a subset of sites (news, health, jobs). Everything *outside* the app incurs normal fees. Offered across many developing regions under the [Internet.org / Free Basics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet.org) initiative. Obvious worry: it skews what users read toward the blessed set. **The canonical current-events anchor:** India *banned* it in Feb 2016 on net-neutrality grounds — TRAI's [Prohibition of Discriminatory Tariffs for Data Services Regulations, 2016](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_neutrality_in_India) — see also [Davis Wright Tremaine's contemporaneous analysis](https://www.dwt.com/insights/2016/02/india-rejects-facebooks-zerorating-free-basics-pro). TRAI's reasoning: allowing price differentiation based on content type would "militate against the very basis on which the internet has developed." Facebook board member Marc Andreessen's reaction ("anti-colonialist") backfired publicly. India remains one of the strictest zero-rating jurisdictions in the world. |
| **India Bans Free Basics (2016)** | The canonical developing-world counterpoint — it predates both the US pendulum and the EU/CJEU rulings. On **Feb 8, 2016** India's regulator **TRAI** issued the **Prohibition of Discriminatory Tariffs for Data Services Regulations** ("no service provider shall offer or charge discriminatory tariffs for data services on the basis of content"), killing Free Basics and Airtel Zero outright — Rs 50,000/day penalty, emergency-services carve-out. See [Net neutrality in India (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_neutrality_in_India). The framing to land: a country with genuinely expensive data — exactly the "walled garden may beat *nothing*" case — still chose to prohibit it, on the view that a curated free slice skews the information ecosystem. It bans zero rating on the **price** side, where the EU later did on the **commercial-discrimination** side; the market Free Basics was built *for* rejected it. |
| **Walled Garden, or Gateway Drug?** | Central empirical question — does zero rating *trap* users or *onboard* them? Book is careful: depends on the *counterfactual*. Where data is otherwise unaffordable, a walled garden may beat *nothing*; where it isn't, it locks users into a few dominant platforms. |
| **A Natural Experiment: Cell C Zero-Rates WhatsApp** | Longitudinal MySpeedTest study (Kenya / South Africa). Cellular WhatsApp usage roughly tripled after Cell C zero-rated it; effect appeared on Wi-Fi too — users treated the app as "free to use anywhere." Small sample (~19 users) — flag the small-N caveat honestly; it's a teaching moment about measurement (preview Ch. 5). |
| **A Natural Experiment: MTN Zero-Rates Twitter (2014 World Cup)** | MTN zero-rated Twitter May-Jul 2014 (red bars in the chart mark the window). Cellular usage picked up with a slight lag, peaked near the end, stayed elevated for months. Persistence-after-promotion is the interesting result: not pure short-term substitution. By year-end returned to baseline — so *durable* effect is partial. |
| **What Users Told Us** | Surveys in Kenya and South Africa. Cuts against a strong walled-garden story — users who started via zero rating often moved to paid full-service plans. But "free" still measurably bends behavior. Both true — that tension is the point. |
| **A 2025 Vignette: Europe Bars Zero Rating** | **Marquee current-event case:** CJEU **C-367/24**, judgment of **10 July 2025** (Romanian "unlimited internet bonus"). Read the judgment: [EUR-Lex](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/PDF/?uri=CELEX:62024CJ0367) · [EUR-Lex case page](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=celex:62024CJ0367) · [Bird & Bird analysis](https://www.twobirds.com/en/insights/2025/eu-court-issues-further-guidance-on-net-neutrality-and-zero-rating) · [EDRi analysis](https://edri.org/our-work/cjeu-in-surprise-judgment-zero-rating-is-illegal-under-eu-law/). Reinforces the CJEU's consistent 2020-2021 line: zero-rating tariffs violate [Regulation (EU) 2015/2120](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2015/2120/oj) because they discriminate on **commercial** rather than technical grounds. Even customer-activated options with equal treatment among non-partners still violate the OIR when they impose category-specific restrictions (like video bandwidth caps). Combined with [BEREC net-neutrality guidelines](https://www.berec.europa.eu/en/document-categories/berec/regulatory-best-practices/guidelines/berec-guidelines-on-the-implementation-of-the-open-internet-regulation), zero rating is effectively off the table across the EU — even as the US has *no* federal net-neutrality rules at all. **The transatlantic split is the teaching gold.** BEREC's ongoing [5G network-slicing consultation (2026)](https://www.berec.europa.eu/en/public-consultations-calls-for-inputs/public-consultation-on-the-draft-berec-further-guidance-on-5g-network-slicing?language_content_entity=en) is the next front — same question in wireless (see the 5G Slicing row).<br><br>**Freshest EU development — the fight is reopening:** on **Jan 21, 2026** the Commission tabled a draft **Digital Networks Act (DNA)** that would fold the Open Internet Regulation into a single directly-applicable regulation. It drops a *mandatory* "fair share" network fee, but digital-rights groups warn it reopens a path to network fees and to Commission-set "specialised services" (i.e., paid fast lanes) while diluting BEREC's independence: [EDRi — The EU Commission is gutting net neutrality (Feb 2026)](https://edri.org/our-work/the-eu-commission-is-gutting-net-neutrality/). Through-line for class: the EU **zero-rating ban is settled**, but the **fast-lane and fair-share fights are reopening** on the European side — just as the US has no rules at all. |
| **Takeaways** | Land the through-line: control doesn't need a firewall. A price difference most people won't pay to escape is enough to shape what a population reads and uses. **Bridge to demonetization as economic censorship** — the platform-layer analogue of zero rating: not blocked, just unprofitable, therefore invisible. **Recent current-event anchors students may raise:** (1) **YouTube's July 15, 2025 renaming** of "repetitious content" → **"inauthentic content"**, targeting AI "slop": [TorrentFreak overview](https://torrentfreak.com/youtube-processed-2-5-billion-content-id-copyright-claims-in-2025/) and the [Contrails.ai policy analysis](https://www.contrails.ai/blogs/youtube-moves-curb-ai-slop-wakeup-call-creators). Enforcement examples: Screen Culture and KH Studio channels (2M+ combined subs) terminated in Dec 2025 for AI-generated fake movie trailers. (2) **YouTube Adpocalypse 2017** — the mass-advertiser boycott that triggered algorithmic demonetization: [Adpocalypse (Wikitubia)](https://youtube.fandom.com/wiki/Adpocalypse). (3) **August 2019 LGBTQ+ creators lawsuit** (Divino Group et al. v. Google/YouTube) alleging systematic demonetization of trans/queer content: [*Rolling Stone*](https://www.rollingstone.com/culture/culture-features/lgbtq-youtube-lawsuit-censorship-877919/) · [*Forbes* on the filing](https://www.forbes.com/sites/rachelsandler/2019/08/14/lgbtq-creators-sue-youtube-for-alleged-discrimination/). One plaintiff's monthly revenue reportedly fell from $3,500 to $500. (4) **TikTok Creator Fund → Creator Rewards Program** (March 2024): [TikTok support page](https://support.tiktok.com/en/business-and-creator/tiktok-creator-fund-us/tiktok-creator-fund-update-us) — new program requires videos >1 minute, changing what content gets monetized and effectively demonetizing short-form. (5) **YouTube Partner Program 2025 expansion** lowered the entry bar to 500 subs / 3,000 watch hours or 3M Shorts views for fan-funding tools — see [YouTube Creators page](https://www.youtube.com/creators/earn/youtube-partner-program/). (6) **Pew Research (Jun 24, 2025)**, "Few Americans pay for news when they encounter paywalls" — **74%** encounter paywalls, only **1%** pay: [Pew short-read](https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2025/06/24/few-americans-pay-for-news-when-they-encounter-paywalls/). Cleanest recent evidence that a soft price barrier is a hard access barrier — direct analog to zero rating's "tax on attention." |
| **Payment Rails as the New Choke Point** | The purest analog of zero rating at the *stack* level: not blocking, just refusing to process the transaction. If nobody can pay you, your content is effectively demonetized — a "tax on the seller" that reaches all the way to the wallet. **Marquee 2025 case: Steam + Itch.io mass NSFW purge, July 2025.** After the Australian pressure group [**Collective Shout** sent an open letter to Visa, Mastercard, PayPal, Discover, Paysafe, and JCB](https://aftermath.site/steam-itch-porn-censorship-collective-shout-visa-mastercard-paypal/), Valve pulled hundreds of adult titles from Steam and [Itch.io de-indexed all NSFW content on July 24, 2025](https://www.thesixthaxis.com/2025/07/24/itch-io-delists-all-adult-nsfw-content-due-to-pressure-from-payment-processors-and-collective-shout/) ("loss of payment methods would prevent customers from being able to purchase other titles"). Same lever [used against Pornhub Dec 2020](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Financial_censorship) and against [OnlyFans in Aug 2021](https://edition.cnn.com/2021/08/20/tech/onlyfans-explicit-content-ban-payment/index.html); a [2024 whistleblower complaint](https://www.theglobeandmail.com/world/article-mastercard-visa-failed-to-stop-payments-for-child-sex-abuse-material/) alleged the card networks knew but didn't act. **Political-speech variant:** [FTC Chair Ferguson sent enforcement warning letters to Visa, Mastercard, PayPal, and Stripe in 2025](https://www.bankingdive.com/news/ftc-threatens-enforcement-action-debanking-visa-mastercard-paypal-stripe/815969/) accusing them of debanking customers on ideological grounds. **Distribution-layer variant:** [Google Podcasts shut down April 2, 2024](https://techcrunch.com/2023/09/26/google-podcasts-to-shut-down-in-2024-with-listeners-migrated-to-youtube-music/), migrating listeners to YouTube Music — a soft-throttle on podcast discovery. **Substack's Nazi-content controversy Jan 2024** ([Platformer's departure post](https://www.platformer.news/substack-says-it-will-remove-nazi/), [CNN](https://www.cnn.com/2024/01/09/tech/substack-removes-newsletters-for-pro-nazi-content)) — Substack removed five newsletters but refused to proactively moderate, prompting Casey Newton (Platformer) to leave the platform. The choke point isn't the network — it's the rail (Visa), the store (Steam), the app (Podcasts), and the newsletter platform (Substack). |
| **Next: Measuring Information Control** | Bridge to Ch. 5. Comcast/Netflix showed how hard it is to tell throttling from congestion from a peering dispute. That's exactly why we need the measurement toolkit ([OONI](https://ooni.org/), [M-Lab](https://www.measurementlab.net/), [Censored Planet](https://censoredplanet.org/)) coming up. |
