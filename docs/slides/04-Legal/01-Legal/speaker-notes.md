# Lecture 7 — Speaker Notes (Law as Information Control)

Per-slide context + clickable links. In-deck notes (the `::: {.notes}` blocks) are also visible via reveal.js speaker view (press **S**). Every URL was verified this session or is a canonical org landing page.

| Slide | Context + Links |
|---|---|
| **Behind Every Technical Control Is a Law** | Book Ch. 4, §4.1 opening. The whole deck reframes: the state usually doesn't need to run a DPI box if it can make a platform *afraid of liability*. Law is a lever that moves the platform; the platform moves the content. Roberts's "tax on access" framing (Ch. 1) transposed from packets to legal exposure. Jack Balkin's ["Old School / New School Speech Regulation" (Harvard L. Rev. 2014)](https://law.yale.edu/sites/default/files/area/center/isp/documents/jack_balkin_-_old_school_new_school_speech_regulation_-_fesc.pdf) is the theoretical anchor — old-school = criminal penalties on speakers; new-school = pressure on intermediaries. |
| **The Central Question** | This is the spine of the whole deck. Balkin's "collateral censorship" idea: strict liability pushes platforms to over-remove because it's cheaper to take down than defend. Book §4.1. Full immunity, by contrast, leaves harmful content up with no accountability. Every regime in this lecture is a different answer to this one trade-off — DMCA, §230, DSA, jawboning cases. |
| **Law Taxes Speech Without Banning It** | Tie back to the Fear / Friction / Flooding taxonomy from Roberts. Copyright and defamation are the paradigm cases of *cost-as-censorship*: a notice is cheaper to obey than to fight. Balkin's ["Free Speech Is a Triangle" (Columbia L. Rev. 2018)](https://columbialawreview.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/11/Balkin-FREE_SPEECH_IS_A_TRIANGLE.pdf) — governments, platforms, and speakers form a triangle where pressure on any corner reshapes the others. |
| **CDA §230: The 26 Words That Made the Internet** | Statutory text: [47 U.S.C. §230](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/47/230). The "26 words" framing is from Jeff Kosseff's [*The Twenty-Six Words That Created the Internet* (Cornell UP, 2019)](https://www.cornellpress.cornell.edu/book/9781501714412/the-twenty-six-words-that-created-the-internet/). §230 is *why* user-generated content at scale exists — policing billions of posts for liability would be impossible. Political framing: the right says it lets platforms suppress conservative views with immunity; the left says it lets platforms profit from harm without accountability. Book §4.1. |
| **Reno v. ACLU (1997): The Internet Gets Free-Speech Rights** | [*Reno v. ACLU*, 521 U.S. 844 (1997)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/521/844/) — first Supreme Court ruling on regulating Internet content. Doctrinal move: the Internet is like a printing press (high protection), not like a TV station (heavily regulated scarcity-era medium). Why the US baseline is so speech-protective compared to the EU. Preserved narrow carve-outs (obscenity, CSAM). |
| **FOSTA/SESTA (2018): The First Crack in §230** | The first major carve-out in two decades. [FOSTA-SESTA (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FOSTA-SESTA). Removes §230 immunity for sites that knowingly facilitate sex trafficking; aimed at [backpage.com](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Backpage). Book §4.1's deep point: once you make a platform liable, you implicitly mandate the detection technology — only big platforms can afford that, so liability rules *consolidate market power*. The **newest US carve-out is the TAKE IT DOWN Act** (signed **May 19, 2025**): platforms must remove nonconsensual intimate images, including AI deepfakes, within **48 hours** of a valid notice — [full text S.146](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/146) · [CRS explainer LSB11314](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/LSB11314) · [FTC enforcement began May 2026](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/blog/2026/05/take-it-down-act-enforcement-starts-now-what-know-about-ftc-tida). Same "notice cheaper than fight" dynamic that drives DMCA over-removal, now for intimate images. |
| **§230 Reform: Everyone Hates It, Nobody Fixes It** | Reform proposals collide with hard problems: "neutral" is undefinable, *everything* is algorithmically ranked, more carve-outs create a slippery slope. Despite endless rhetoric, §230 is essentially unchanged since FOSTA/SESTA — TAKE IT DOWN adds a content-specific removal duty but doesn't touch §230 immunity. Weakening §230 pushes platforms toward "when in doubt, take it down" — shrinking speech. Consensus is impossible because left and right want opposite fixes. |
| **Who Decides? Platforms vs. Government** | **Moody v. NetChoice (2024):** [Supreme Court opinion](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/23pdf/22-277_d18f.pdf) — Texas HB20 / Florida SB7072 banning "viewpoint-based" moderation; Court held platform curation is expressive, First-Amendment protected. Remanded for feature-specific analysis; as of mid-2026 still in district courts. **Murthy v. Missouri (2024):** [Supreme Court opinion](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/23pdf/23-411_3dq3.pdf) — did the government *coerce* platforms to remove COVID/election content? Court ducked on *standing* — the jawboning line stays unsettled. Book §4.1. These are the two poles: can the state force platforms to keep speech *up*, and can it pressure them to take speech *down*? |
| **State "No-Viewpoint" Laws: The Implementation Problem** | Texas HB20 and Florida SB7072 (both 2021) ban "viewpoint-based" moderation. Set aside the First Amendment fight ([Moody v. NetChoice](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/23pdf/22-277_d18f.pdf) held platform curation is presumptively protected, then remanded — as of mid-2026 still in the district courts): the laws are hard to *operate*. **(1) Geolocation** — they apply to users *in* Texas/Florida, but IP geolocation is imprecise (worst near borders) and users travel, so "which state's rule governs this post" has no crisp answer. **(2) Viewpoint vs. policy** — is "anti-vaccine-mandate" a viewpoint or a policy violation? The line is wide enough to drive a truck through, and Florida attaches **$250k/day** fines for deplatforming a statewide candidate. Book §4.1 "state-level content moderation laws." |
| **Intermediary Liability Is Not Universal** | §230 is a **US** rule — global baseline varies sharply. EU **e-Commerce Directive** ([2000/31/EC](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX%3A32000L0031)) → now the [**Digital Services Act**](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/digital-services-act-package): conditional liability + duties. A platform's global removal behavior tends to be set by the *strictest* regime it must obey — sets up the Brussels effect slide. |
| **Age-Gating: Excluding a Whole Audience** | A newer lever: remove an entire *class of speakers*, not the speech. Australia's **Online Safety Amendment (Social Media Minimum Age) Act** took effect **Dec 10, 2025** — the world's first under-16 social-media ban, enforced by the [eSafety Commissioner](https://www.esafety.gov.au/), fines up to **AUD $50M**, and notably **no parental-consent carve-out** (the state overrides the parent). Early figure: ~**4.7M** under-16 accounts removed by mid-December 2025. Implementation mirrors the state-law slide: age verification needs IDs or AI face-estimation (a *privacy tax*), can't reliably resolve the 15-vs-16 boundary, and is circumvented by VPNs / borrowed accounts. The UK plus Denmark, Norway, Spain, France, Italy and several US states are exploring similar rules. Refresh the 4.7M figure and ban status annually. Book §4.1 "age-gating and youth social media restrictions." |
| **DMCA: Two Distinct Levers** | Statutory text: [17 U.S.C. §512](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/17/512) and [17 U.S.C. §1201](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/17/1201) (anti-circumvention). §512 is the template for the modern takedown economy — send a notice, content comes down, burden of restoring falls on the poster (counter-notice). **The asymmetry is the censorship risk.** WSJ's May 2020 investigation ("Google's Takedown Notices") found *hundreds* of legitimate news articles removed via fraudulent DMCA notices, prompting Google to restore 52,000+ links and identify 100+ abusive submitters (Wall Street Journal, May 2020). Google's own [Copyright Transparency Report](https://transparencyreport.google.com/copyright?hl=en) — cumulative removal requests are now in the multi-billion range. |
| **The $1 Billion Question: Cox v. Sony** | **March 25, 2026 — SCOTUS reversed the $1B verdict against Cox.** Read the opinion: [24-171, *Cox Communications v. Sony Music Entertainment*](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/25pdf/24-171_bq7d.pdf). Also: [SCOTUSblog case file](https://www.scotusblog.com/cases/case-files/cox-communications-inc-v-sony-music-entertainment/) · [Wiley alert on the ruling](https://www.wiley.law/alert-Supreme-Court-Reverses-Cox-Communications-Copyright-Infringement-Liability-Ruling-in-Landmark-Unanimous-Decision) · [Sidley analysis](https://www.sidley.com/en/insights/newsupdates/2026/03/us-supreme-court-clarifies-standard-for-contributory-copyright-liability). Justice Thomas for a 7-Justice majority: "a company is not liable as a copyright infringer for merely providing a service to the public with knowledge that it will be used by some to infringe." Sotomayor concurred but warned the majority "unnecessarily limits secondary liability" and "upends the statutory incentive structure that Congress created." Teaching point: the threat of liability was pushing ISPs to terminate users on the basis of unproven notices — exactly the over-removal dynamic §512 encourages. After the decision, SCOTUS vacated the Fifth Circuit's similar ruling in *Grande Communications v. UMG Recordings*. |
| **Copyright Weaponized to Silence Critics** | The classic academic-freedom case: **Felten/SDMI (2001)** — RIAA threatened DMCA suit to block security research, backed down after EFF + outcry ([EFF summary of Felten v. RIAA](https://www.eff.org/cases/felten-et-al-v-riaa-et-al)). SIGCOMM example: research community using copyright to silence a review-process critic (descriptive citation, no confirmed URL). **The EFF Takedown Hall of Shame** catalogues the abuse pattern: [eff.org/takedowns](https://www.eff.org/takedowns) — Prince's Raspberry Beret Lifetime Aggrievement Award, Fox's automated bots hitting Cory Doctorow's novel *Homeland*, Doane University silencing faculty protest, Dr. Drew Pinsky using DMCA to hide criticism. **Recent current events:** the **YouTube 2025 Content ID transparency report** processed **2.5 billion claims** in 2025 — a 14% YoY increase — with abuse concentrated in the webform: "the attempted abuse rate through the webform was more than 10 times higher than across all other copyright removal tools" — see [TorrentFreak summary](https://torrentfreak.com/youtube-processed-2-5-billion-content-id-copyright-claims-in-2025/) and the [Google Transparency Report](https://transparencyreport.google.com/youtube-copyright/everyone-has-access). Fair use protects the speech *in principle*, but the takedown happens first and the fight comes later.<br><br>**Generative-AI copyright frontier (live in 2026):** the same fair-use question now dominates AI-training litigation. In the consolidated *In re OpenAI Copyright Infringement Litigation* (SDNY), the court ordered OpenAI to hand over **20 million de-identified ChatGPT logs** to the news plaintiffs because they "bear on OpenAI's fair use defense" — [National Law Review on the Jan 5, 2026 discovery ruling](https://natlawreview.com/article/openai-loses-privacy-gambit-20-million-chatgpt-logs-likely-headed-copyright). Then on **July 9, 2026** the NYT-led group asked the court to **sanction OpenAI** for allegedly concealing and destroying evidence of how ChatGPT was trained on their articles — [Tech Xplore / AP (July 2026)](https://techxplore.com/news/2026-07-news-outlets-urge-sanction-openai.html). The core dispute is whether training on copyrighted news is transformative fair use or unfair competition; the NYT reports having spent **$28M+** litigating against AI companies. |
| **The CFAA and the Chilling of Research** | The **Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (1986)** bans access "without authorization" or "exceeding authorized access" — vague enough to threaten the measurement work in this course (bridge to Ch. 5). ***Sandvig v. Barr* (D.D.C. 2020):** researchers who wanted to audit hiring/housing algorithms for discrimination sued preemptively on First Amendment grounds; the court held that violating a site's terms of service is *not* by itself a CFAA crime. ***Van Buren v. United States*, 593 U.S. 374 (2021):** the Supreme Court read "exceeds authorized access" *narrowly* — misusing access you legitimately have is not automatically a federal crime, relief for security researchers. Yet ambiguity still chills: the book's figures — ~**40%** of security researchers avoid some vulnerability work for fear of legal threats, **>20%** have been threatened — show the law need not convict anyone to suppress accountability research. Background: [EFF — Computer Fraud and Abuse Act reform](https://www.eff.org/issues/cfaa). Book §4.1 "Computer Fraud and Abuse Act." |
| **SLAPP: Suing People into Silence** | Real cease-and-desist Feamster received over a Google review, threatening $250k in damages. Purest illustration of the deck's thesis: no court ruling, no illegal speech — just cost as a censorship tool. **Anti-SLAPP statutes** let defendants get these dismissed early and recover fees; coverage is patchy across US states. [Public Participation Project state-by-state tracker](https://anti-slapp.org/your-states-free-speech-protection). Federal proposal: SLAPP Protection Act (reintroduced in each recent Congress) has not passed. Book §4.1. Related recent copyright-as-censorship arc: **Anna's Archive** hit with a **$322M default judgment** (April 14, 2026, SDNY, over Spotify scraping) and a **$19.5M default judgment** (May 2026, publishers) — orders included global domain-registrar takedown obligations against Cloudflare, Public Interest Registry, Tucows and others: [TorrentFreak on $322M ruling](https://torrentfreak.com/annas-archive-loses-322-million-spotify-piracy-case-without-a-fight/) · [TorrentFreak on $19.5M ruling](https://torrentfreak.com/annas-archive-hit-with-19-5m-default-judgment-and-global-domain-takedown-order/). Whether any money is *collected* is another question — the operators remain anonymous. |
| **The DSA: Regulate the Process, Not the Speech** | **DSA** in force **Nov 16, 2022**, fully applicable **Feb 17, 2024**: [European Commission DSA page](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/digital-services-act-package). Fundamentally different philosophy from US state laws — the US fights over *whether* platforms may moderate; the EU regulates *how visibly and accountably* they moderate. VLOP/VLOSE threshold: 45M+ EU monthly users (~10% of the EU); current [list of designated VLOPs/VLOSEs](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/list-designated-vlops-and-vloses). Penalties up to **6% of global annual revenue**. |
| **Statements of Reasons + the Transparency Database** | The DSA's signature innovation. Before the DSA, platforms self-reported in incompatible formats, omitting who requested takedowns and whether decisions were automated. Every moderation action now gets a machine-readable Statement of Reasons filed to the [**DSA Transparency Database**](https://transparency.dsa.ec.europa.eu/). First time we get standardized, comparable, verifiable moderation data across platforms. Preview of Ch. 5 measurement. |
| **How Big Is the Database?** | 9.4 billion SoRs in roughly the first six months of full operation (as of Jan 20, 2025) — [DSA Transparency Database dashboard](https://transparency.dsa.ec.europa.eu/) is refreshed continuously. The sheer volume is itself a teaching point: moderation at this scale is overwhelmingly automated, which is exactly why the "automated vs. human" field matters. Refresh this figure annually from the live dashboard. |
| **What the DSA Still Misses** | Progress on removals; largely blind to subtler modern control forms — **shadow bans / downranking** and **demonetization** (economic censorship) live in the recommender, which the SoR regime barely touches. **Recent evidence of demonetization as a governance lever:** YouTube's July 15, 2025 renaming of "repetitious content" → **"inauthentic content"** policy explicitly targets AI-generated "slop" — [TorrentFreak summary of YouTube's crackdown](https://torrentfreak.com/youtube-processed-2-5-billion-content-id-copyright-claims-in-2025/) and the [Contrails.ai analysis](https://www.contrails.ai/blogs/youtube-moves-curb-ai-slop-wakeup-call-creators). Demonetization is pure economic censorship and the DSA does not capture it. Enforcement is only as good as the Commission's will to fine — and the first cases have now landed.<br><br>**DSA enforcement update (relevant to today's X presentation):** on **Dec 5, 2025** the Commission issued its **first-ever DSA fine — €120M against X** — for a misleading "blue check" verification system, opaque ad transparency, and blocking researcher data access ([IAPP, Dec 2025](https://iapp.org/news/a/european-commission-fines-x-120m-euros-for-dsa-violations)). Separately, on **Jan 26, 2026** the Commission opened a formal proceeding into **X's integration of the Grok chatbot** — whether X assessed and mitigated systemic risks (including AI-manipulated sexual images and CSAM) before deployment — and extended its existing recommender-systems probe ([European Commission, Jan 2026](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/news/commission-investigates-grok-and-xs-recommender-systems-under-digital-services-act)). TikTok, by contrast, avoided a fine by making binding ad-transparency commitments. Both are exactly the "does the Commission actually bite" test — now answered for X. |
| **The Brussels (and California) Effect** | The "Brussels effect" is Anu Bradford's ([Columbia Law](https://www.law.columbia.edu/faculty/anu-bradford)) framework — see her [book at Oxford UP](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/the-brussels-effect-9780190088583). Same dynamic gave us GDPR globally; **California** does it within the US (CCPA/CPRA). Region-specific compliance is expensive, geolocation is imprecise, so it's easier to ship one global standard — EU sets the de facto baseline. Normative worry: should EU regulators or California voters set speech rules for users in other countries? Evidence on whether platforms truly globalize DSA features is still mixed. |
| **Takeaways** | Synthesis: every regime here prices speech differently. **US = immunity + speech rights** (§230, Reno, Moody); **EU = transparency + duties** (DSA); **copyright/SLAPP = cost-as-weapon** (DMCA §512, Anna's Archive judgments, EFF Hall of Shame). *Murthy* leaves the persuasion/coercion line unsettled. Bridge to next deck: from *law* as tax on speech to *economics* as tax on access — net neutrality, zero rating, and demonetization. |
