| **A Tax on Access, Not a Wall** | The "friction is a form of control" frame is from Roberts, [*Censored*](https://press.princeton.edu/books/hardcover/9780691178868/censored) (Princeton, 2018). Book context: §2.3, "Disrupting the Flow of Data." |
| **Two Ways to Degrade, Not Block** | Both are *friction* mechanisms — no block page, no ban. If a student asks "why isn't this outright blocking?" — the book defines information control as any *tax* on access. |
| **Throttling Is Friction by Design** | Rate limiting is universally dual-use — every prod system does it for fairness / SLAs / DoS protection. The censorship use is the same mechanism aimed at particular targets. Book §2.3.1. |
| **How a Traffic Shaper Works** | Identify → shape is the canonical model. Real-world enabler worth naming: **Russia's TSPU** boxes (deployed by Roskomnadzor at ISP interconnects since 2019) — the hardware substrate that made 2024's YouTube throttling possible. See the [DFRLab Jan 2025 deep dive](https://dfrlab.org/2025/01/31/how-russia-throttled-youtube-for-domestic-audiences/) which reverse-engineers the TSPU rules. Commercial vendor to know: **Sandvine** (marketed to ISPs and states alike). |
| **Identifying What to Throttle** | SNI-based and DNS-based identification are what QUIC + [DoH](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc8484) + [TLS ECH](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/draft-ietf-tls-esni) are designed to defeat. Ties to Wednesday's Protocols lecture. Russian TSPU throttled YouTube specifically by SNI-matching `googlevideo.com` and dropping TCP packets — see [Meduza's Dec 20 2024 report](https://meduza.io/en/news/2024/12/20/russia-appears-to-intensify-youtube-throttling-possibly-in-connection-with-putin-s-year-end-press-conference) on how intensity escalated around Putin's press conference. |
| **Shaping: Leaky Bucket vs. Token Bucket** | CS textbook material. Primers: [Leaky bucket (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leaky_bucket), [Token bucket (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Token_bucket). Real deployments use token bucket because burstiness matters for UX. |
| **Why Throttling Is So Hard to Catch** | **Wehe** (Northeastern's throttling detector): <https://wehe.meddle.mobi/>. Paper: Li et al., "A Large-Scale Analysis of Deployed Traffic Differentiation Practices" (SIGCOMM 2019). **Netflix ↔ Comcast 2014** peering-vs-throttling saga: [Comcast–Netflix dispute (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comcast%E2%80%93Netflix_dispute) — foreshadows net neutrality (Ch. 4). |
| **Throttling as a Live Political Tool** | **Marquee current-event case: Russia's slow-motion YouTube kill (2024–25).** Started late July 2024; by Jan 2025 traffic had collapsed from 43% → 6–12% of Russian internet. **15M users lost** by April 2025. Sources: [Carnegie Endowment (Feb 2025) — "Throttling of YouTube Shows That Russia Is Getting Better at Online Censorship"](https://carnegieendowment.org/russia-eurasia/politika/2025/02/russia-youtube-block-attempt?lang=en) · [Wikipedia — Blocking of YouTube in Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blocking_of_YouTube_in_Russia) · [Meduza — from YouTube to "boob tube"](https://meduza.io/en/feature/2025/08/07/from-youtube-to-boob-tube) (Aug 2025) · [Freedom House — Russia FoTN 2025](https://freedomhouse.org/country/russia/freedom-net/2025). Structural data anchor: [Access Now KeepItOn 2025 report](https://www.accessnow.org/internet-shutdowns-2025/) — **313 shutdowns / 52 countries** (up from 296 in 2024); India leads at 65 events. Turkey throttling around unrest/elections: [OONI Turkey](https://explorer.ooni.org/country/TR). |
| **Throttling at Home: A Private "Tax on Access"** | The story that broke it: [Washington Post, Aug 15 2023 — Twitter throttles links to Threads, Bluesky and NYT](https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/2023/08/15/twitter-x-links-delayed/). Follow-ups: [Variety](https://variety.com/2023/digital/news/elon-musk-x-twitter-delay-links-meta-new-york-times-1235697091/) · [Rappler/Reuters](https://www.rappler.com/technology/social-media/x-delays-access-content-reuters-new-york-times-social-media-rivals/). Mechanism: X used its `t.co` redirect to insert ~5 sec of latency. After the Post ran, NYT/Reuters delays quietly vanished; Bluesky/Substack persisted. |
| **DoS: Exhaust a Finite Resource** | Three-resource taxonomy (network / connections / server) is the book's frame. Distinguish DoS (one source) vs. DDoS (many). Mirai/IoT made DDoS cheap; deeper botnet coverage in Ch. 3. |
| **Why DoS Is Hard to Defend: Asymmetry** | Asymmetry / spoofing / indistinguishability — the book's teaching triad. Every defense on the "Defending Against DoS" slide attacks one of the three. |
| **SYN Flood: Asymmetry in TCP** | Attack + mitigations: [RFC 4987 — TCP SYN Flooding Attacks and Common Mitigations](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc4987) (2007). SYN cookies original writeup: [D.J. Bernstein — SYN cookies](https://cr.yp.to/syncookies.html). Summary: [SYN flood (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SYN_flood). |
| **Reflection and Amplification** | Canonical case: **Spamhaus, March 2013, ~300 Gbps via open DNS resolvers** — [DDoS attack on Spamhaus (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DDoS_attack_on_Spamhaus). Modern rhyme: **GitHub took 1.35 Tbps via memcached (Feb 2018)** — [GitHub incident report](https://github.blog/2018-03-01-ddos-incident-report/) — ~50,000× amplification. Defense: [RFC 2827 / BCP 38 — Ingress Filtering](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc2827) (Ferguson & Senie, 2000; still under-deployed). |
| **When DoS Becomes Censorship Infrastructure** | Landmark research: Marczak et al., "An Analysis of China's 'Great Cannon'" (FOCI 2015). Citizen Lab page: [China's Great Cannon](https://citizenlab.ca/2015/04/chinas-great-cannon/). Targets: **GitHub** (hosting GreatFire and NYT-China mirrors) and **GreatFire.org**. Mechanism: on-path, co-located with the GFW; injected JavaScript into Baidu responses to turn external browsers into an attack botnet. |
| **Whose Browsers Got Hijacked?** | The Taiwan / Hong Kong concentration chart on this slide is from the Citizen Lab paper. Teaching point: *the weapon was built from innocent bystanders* — anyone outside China loading Baidu-hosted analytics. |
| **Centralization Makes One Target Enough** | **Dyn attack, Oct 21 2016**: [DDoS attack on Dyn (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DDoS_attack_on_Dyn). Botnet mechanics: [Mirai (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirai_(malware)). Academic anchor: Antonakakis et al., ["Understanding the Mirai Botnet"](https://www.usenix.org/conference/usenixsecurity17/technical-sessions/presentation/antonakakis) (USENIX Security 2017). **Modern rhyme — same centralization, no attack needed**: [Cloudflare's Nov 18 2025 outage](https://blog.cloudflare.com/18-november-2025-outage/) — internal misconfig took X, ChatGPT, Shopify, Truth Social, NJ Transit dark for hours. |
| **Defending Against DoS** | **Marquee current-event data: 2025 was the year DDoS went hyper-scale.** Cloudflare mitigated a **31.4 Tbps** attack in Nov 2025 — largest ever disclosed — lasting just 35 sec. Source: [Cloudflare Q4 2025 DDoS Threat Report](https://blog.cloudflare.com/ddos-threat-report-2025-q4/). The **Aisuru** botnet (1–4M compromised IoT devices, mostly Android TVs and home routers) drove both this and an earlier **29.7 Tbps** record — see [The Hacker News writeup](https://thehackernews.com/2025/12/record-297-tbps-ddos-attack-linked-to.html). Full-year 2025: **47.1 M DDoS attacks** (up 121% YoY); traffic against AI companies **+347% in Sep 2025 alone**. Concept students haven't seen: **anycast** — one IP advertised from many locations; BGP delivers to nearest. All 13 DNS root "servers" are anycast constellations. [Anycast (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anycast). Scrubbing services: Cloudflare Magic Transit, Akamai Prolexic, Radware, Neustar UltraDDoS. Booter/stresser law-enforcement front: [Europol Operation PowerOFF](https://www.europol.europa.eu/media-press/newsroom/news/law-enforcement-shuts-down-27-online-platforms-used-for-launching-ddos-attacks) (Dec 2024, ongoing). |
| **Who Gets to Stay Online?** | 108.9 B / 241% figures: Cloudflare's [Project Galileo 11th anniversary post (Jun 2025)](https://blog.cloudflare.com/celebrating-11-years-of-project-galileo-global-impact/) and the [Cloudflare 5th Annual Impact Report (Dec 19 2025)](https://www.cloudflare.com/press/press-releases/2025/cloudflare-publishes-fifth-annual-impact-report/). Two Galileo case studies worth naming: **Belarusian Investigative Center** — onboarded Sep 27 2024 mid-attack; 28 B requests the next day. **Tech4Peace** — 12-day attack from Mar 10 2025, 2.7 B requests, adaptive intensity.<br><br>**The unelected-arbiter arc — Cloudflare's three inflection points, in more detail:**<br><br>**(1) Daily Stormer — Aug 16, 2017.** Neo-Nazi website that celebrated the [Unite the Right rally in Charlottesville](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unite_the_Right_rally) (where Heather Heyer was killed on Aug 12) and doxxed her mother. Daily Stormer's operators claimed publicly that Cloudflare was a secret supporter — that was the tipping point. Prince terminated them and simultaneously published [Why We Terminated Daily Stormer](https://blog.cloudflare.com/why-we-terminated-daily-stormer/) with the canonical quote: *"Literally, I woke up in a bad mood and decided someone shouldn't be allowed on the Internet. No one should have that power."* Prince also flagged the deeper worry: Cloudflare handles ~10-20% of Internet requests; "a small number of companies will largely determine what can and cannot be online."<br><br>**(2) 8chan — Aug 5, 2019.** Anonymous imageboard descended from 4chan. The Aug 3, 2019 [El Paso Walmart shooter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019_El_Paso_shooting) (23 killed) posted a racist manifesto to 8chan minutes before the attack — as did the Christchurch and Poway shooters that year. Cloudflare terminated 8chan two days later; Prince's blog framed it as an escalation from "distasteful but tolerable" to "actively facilitating mass murder." See [Wikipedia — 8chan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/8chan).<br><br>**(3) Kiwi Farms — Sept 3, 2022.** Message board founded around 2013 to harass a webcomic artist; grew into arguably *the* central hub for coordinating stalking, doxxing, and swatting campaigns against specific people — [three suicides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kiwi_Farms) linked to Kiwi Farms harassment before Cloudflare's termination.<br>*The specific trigger*: In August 2022, Twitch streamer [Clara Sorrenti (Keffals)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clara_Sorrenti), a trans activist, was swatted after Kiwi Farms users organized a doxxing + harassment campaign against her. She fled Canada for Belfast, Ireland; Kiwi Farms users found her there and escalated. She and allies launched a #DropKiwifarms pressure campaign against Cloudflare specifically.<br>*Prince's first response* (Aug 31, 2022) was a **2,600-word blog post defending continued service** — comparing Cloudflare to a phone company that "doesn't terminate your line if you say awful, racist, bigoted things." <br>*The reversal* came **48 hours later**: on Sept 3 Cloudflare cut Kiwi Farms off, citing "an imminent and emergency threat to human life" that had "escalated over the last 48 hours." Prince tweeted the announcement; Kiwi Farms visitors got a Cloudflare error page instead of the site. See [Washington Post — Under pressure, security firm Cloudflare drops Kiwi Farms](https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/2022/09/03/cloudflare-drops-kiwifarms/) and [NBC News](https://www.nbcnews.com/tech/tech-news/cloudflare-provided-security-services-kiwi-farms-blocks-website-rcna46219). Prince didn't disclose *what* changed in those 48 hours, which critics used to argue Cloudflare had folded to activist pressure rather than following a principled rule.<br>*Aftermath*: Kiwi Farms migrated to a smaller CDN (VanwaTech) and has intermittently reappeared. The site is currently reachable but with dramatically reduced traffic and worse infrastructure.<br><br>**Same company, same question, three different answers** — a great classroom debate seed. Note the pattern in the escalation: (1) reputational risk from *false* claim of Cloudflare support; (2) direct link to a *specific* mass-casualty event; (3) sustained *targeted-harassment-of-a-known-victim* campaign. Each felt like the same "who decides" question to Prince and Cloudflare, and each got a slightly different framing from him. |
