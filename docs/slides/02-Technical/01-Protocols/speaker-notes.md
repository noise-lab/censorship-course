# Lecture 2 — Speaker Notes (Disrupting Internet Protocols)

| Slide | Context + Links |
|---|---|
| **Why Are the Internet's Protocols So Easy to Manipulate?** | Book Ch. 2, §2.1. The design-for-trust story is the thesis. The classic paper on the tension is Clark, ["The Design Philosophy of the DARPA Internet Protocols" (SIGCOMM 1988)](https://groups.csail.mit.edu/ana/Publications/PubPDFs/The%20design%20philosophy%20of%20the%20DARPA%20internet%20protocols.pdf) — Clark ranks *seven* design goals; note that *accountability* is last and *survivability* is first. Security is nowhere in the top seven. The [Morris worm (Nov 1988)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morris_worm) was the first wake-up call that trusted-peer assumptions don't hold at Internet scale. The recurring theme: **"the network looks broken" and "the network is being censored" often look identical** — comes back throughout the course, especially Ch. 5 on measurement. |
| **A "Protocol" Is Just an Agreement** | The **"narrow waist" (hourglass model)** is the design principle to name-drop: everything runs *over* IP; IP runs *over* everything. See [RFC 3439 (2002) — Some Internet Architectural Guidelines](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc3439) §2.1 and Steve Deering's ["Watching the Waist of the Protocol Hourglass"](https://www.iab.org/wp-content/IAB-uploads/2011/03/hourglass-london-ietf.pdf) slides. **Postel's Robustness Principle** ("be conservative in what you send, be liberal in what you accept") is the *cultural* explanation for why every protocol is trivially fooled: nothing enforces sanity on inputs — see [RFC 1122](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc1122). Takeaway: "no built-in authentication, no accountability" — the lever every technique in the deck pulls. |
| **Manipulation Targets a Few Points in the Stack** | Book Table 2.1 is the roadmap for the entire lecture — each row = one section of the deck. Aksoy et al., ["A Taxonomy of Internet Censorship and Anti-Censorship" (arXiv 2011)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1108.3646) is an early and still-useful classification along similar lines. Book's own layered model matches the FIRE (Fake / Intercept / Reset / Erase) breakdown popularized by measurement work in the 2010s. |
| **DNS Maps Names to Addresses** | **DNS foundational RFCs:** [RFC 1034 (concepts)](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc1034) and [RFC 1035 (implementation)](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc1035), Paul Mockapetris, 1987 — old but current. **Root-server geography:** [root-servers.org](https://www.root-servers.org/) shows the anycast footprint of the 13 root-server systems. Useful when a student asks "isn't the root a single point of failure?" — answer: each letter is anycast across hundreds of physical sites. Local resolver: [DHCP options (RFC 2132)](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc2132) is how a resolver gets assigned — most people never think about it, but that assignment is exactly the on-path chokepoint censors exploit. |
| **DNS Is Insecure by Default** | **Origin as monetization, not censorship.** The paradigmatic cases: [**Verisign SiteFinder (Sep 2003)**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Site_Finder) — Verisign hijacked `NXDOMAIN` responses for `.com`/`.net` to redirect mistyped domains to a Verisign-owned search/ads page; ICANN forced withdrawal. **Comcast NXDOMAIN redirection (2009)** — ISP-level monetization of typos with an ads page ([DNS hijacking, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNS_hijacking#Manipulation_by_ISPs)). **Paxfire (2011)** — Charter, RCN, Cavalier and other US ISPs intercepted DNS for popular sites and sold the redirected search traffic; see [EFF's writeup](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2011/07/widespread-search-hijacking-in-the-us). **Iran fake root referrals (2013)** documented DNS answers arriving from *inside Iran* faster than the speed of light would allow — timing anomaly = tell; academic follow-up is Anonymous, ["The Collateral Damage of Internet Censorship by DNS Injection," SIGCOMM CCR 2012](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/2317307.2317311). **The historical arc to name in class:** DNS manipulation was invented for *ads*. Censors just noticed the trick worked. |
| **DNS Censorship in the Wild: Turkey, 2014** | **Twitter block (Mar 20, 2014) followed by YouTube block (Mar 27, 2014)** — Erdoğan pre-election crackdown after leaked recordings surfaced on both platforms. The **`8.8.8.8` iconography** — spray-painted on walls in Istanbul and Ankara — became the canonical DNS-censorship image. ISPs escalated: initially blocked at DNS (recursive resolver returned wrong answer), then blocked 8.8.8.8 itself when users switched. See [Censorship of Twitter in Turkey (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Censorship_of_Twitter_in_Turkey). **The lesson:** the *first* layer censors reach for is also the *easiest* to route around — that's part of why it stays popular. Turkey's later moves included the 2017–2019 [Wikipedia block](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Block_of_Wikipedia_in_Turkey). |
| **Detecting DNS Manipulation Is Hard** | **The seminal measurement paper** on multi-signal DNS censorship detection: Pearce et al., ["Global Measurement of DNS Manipulation" (USENIX Security 2017)](https://www.usenix.org/system/files/conference/usenixsecurity17/sec17-pearce.pdf) — the IRIS system, which establishes the AS + content + rDNS + certificate triangulation this slide describes. Follow-on / production: [**Censored Planet**](https://censoredplanet.org/) at U-Michigan (Roya Ensafi's group) — continuous, remote measurement using the Satellite / Iris pipeline. **The CDN confound is the real trap** — Cloudflare, Akamai, Fastly legitimately return different IPs per user location; detection has to distinguish "regional CDN endpoint" from "government redirect." **OONI's DNS-consistency probe** is the community-scale version: [OONI DNS Consistency test](https://ooni.org/nettest/dns-consistency/). |
| **Encrypted DNS Helps — and Centralizes** | **DoH:** [RFC 8484 (2018)](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc8484). Chrome ships Google `8.8.8.8` as default; Firefox ships Cloudflare `1.1.1.1`. **DoT:** [RFC 7858 (2016)](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc7858) — port 853, easier to block than DoH because it's on a distinctive port. **DNSSEC:** [RFC 4033 (2005)](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc4033) — integrity, not confidentiality; root zone signed, most TLDs signed, adoption on individual zones patchy. **ODoH:** [RFC 9230 (2022)](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc9230) — Cloudflare + Apple, splits *who* is asking (proxy) from *what* is being asked (target); [Cloudflare's ODoH announcement](https://blog.cloudflare.com/oblivious-dns/). **Germany 2023 — Quad9 v. Sony Music:** German regional courts ordered [Quad9](https://www.quad9.net/) to block piracy domains at the DNS-resolver layer. **The centralization tension is the modern point:** old world compel 10,000 ISP resolvers; new world compel Cloudflare or Google *once* and you affect millions. That reversal is what makes DoH double-edged. **Cloudflare 1.1.1.1 outage (Jul 14, 2025)** underscored the fragility: [Cloudflare post-mortem](https://blog.cloudflare.com/cloudflare-1-1-1-1-incident-on-july-14-2025/). |
| **TCP in One Slide** | **TCP:** [RFC 793 (1981)](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc793), superseded by [RFC 9293 (2022)](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc9293). **IPv4 exhaustion**, if a student asks: ARIN ran out Sep 2015; RIPE Nov 2019; APNIC Apr 2011 — see [IPv4 exhaustion (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IPv4_address_exhaustion). **The forgeable-RST fact** is the pivot for the next slide — nothing in the TCP spec signs a RST; anyone on-path can craft one that both endpoints will honor. |
| **TCP Reset Injection** | **The canonical academic paper:** Clayton, Murdoch, Watson, ["Ignoring the Great Firewall of China" (PETS 2007)](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-540-75551-7_2) — Cambridge team documented forged RST injection, and showed that *ignoring* the RST at both endpoints defeats it. Xu, Mao, Halderman, "Internet Censorship in China: Where Does the Filtering Occur?" (PAM 2011) locates the GFW's injectors on provincial-backbone routers (AS-level filtering, not per-ISP). Weaver et al., "Detecting Forged TCP Reset Packets" (NDSS 2009) documents duplicate-sequence-number variants. **Commercial DPI tools that do this:** Blue Coat, Sandvine, older Cisco content-filtering appliances. If a student asks whether US networks have this capability: yes, most enterprise firewalls (Palo Alto, Fortinet) can inject resets on policy match — the difference is *policy*, not capability. |
| **Defending TCP/IP: Encryption Moves the Battle** | **TLS 1.3:** [RFC 8446 (2018)](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc8446) — encrypts the certificate; the only cleartext left in the handshake is the SNI. **ECH:** [draft-ietf-tls-esni](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/draft-ietf-tls-esni) closes the SNI leak; see [Cloudflare's Announcing ECH (Sep 2023)](https://blog.cloudflare.com/announcing-encrypted-client-hello/). **States now blocking ECH:** Russia blocked ECH in late 2024 — see [Meduza coverage (Nov 8, 2024)](https://meduza.io/en/news/2024/11/08/russia-s-internet-censor-demands-a-break-from-cloudflare-after-the-american-company-enabled-new-privacy-tools-last-month). China's GFW blocks ECH-configured handshakes — [Great Firewall Report / net4people bbs](https://github.com/net4people/bbs/issues) is a community-run tracker. Iran reportedly experimenting. **VPN / Tor / Shadowsocks primer** — one-liner map: VPN tunnels a whole flow, Tor onions traffic through three relays, Shadowsocks obfuscates SOCKS to look like TLS. **DPI vendors:** Sandvine (Iran, Russia, Egypt), Netsweeper, Blue Coat (now Symantec); [Citizen Lab Sandvine coverage](https://citizenlab.ca/tag/sandvine/). |
| **BGP: Driving Directions, One Hop at a Time** | **BGP:** [RFC 4271 (2006)](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc4271) — the "no security" property is by design; no built-in origin authentication. **Number of ASNs today:** roughly 80,000–100,000 active. Live count at [CIDR Report — Active BGP Entries](https://www.cidr-report.org/as2.0/). **RouteViews** archive: [routeviews.org](https://www.routeviews.org/) — the hands-on for Lecture 3. **RIPE RIS** European equivalent: [ris.ripe.net](https://ris.ripe.net/). **The transitive-trust vulnerability** — a good intuition-builder: a neighbor's neighbor lies; your router accepts the lie because your neighbor said "yes, that's fine." |
| **Route Hijacking: China Telecom, 2010** | The Dyn/Renesys post-mortem "China's 18-Minute Mystery" (Apr 2010) — try the [Web Archive](https://web.archive.org/) if the direct link 404s; summary in the [BGP hijacking notable incidents section (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BGP_hijacking#Notable_incidents). Congressional coverage: [US-China Economic and Security Review Commission 2010 Annual Report](https://www.uscc.gov/annual-report/2010-annual-report-congress) — search "China Telecom" in the PDF. **The "why did most people not notice" cold-call answer** to land in class: (1) China Telecom announced the *same* prefix length, not more-specific, so BGP's best-path selection came down to *shortest AS path* — many routers kept the legitimate route; (2) traffic still reached the destination via a detour — no black hole, just a *surveillable* path. The scary part isn't the outage; it's the silent detour through a state-controlled network. |
| **Hijacks Cut Both Ways: Pakistan & YouTube** | **The 2008 incident:** [RIPE NCC analysis (Feb 2008)](https://www.ripe.net/publications/news/industry-developments/youtube-hijacking-a-ripe-ncc-ris-case-study/) — the definitive technical walkthrough of the 24-minute global YouTube outage caused by Pakistan Telecom trying to block YouTube domestically. **The mechanism:** longest-prefix match — Pakistan advertised `208.65.153.0/24` (more specific than YouTube's `208.65.152.0/22`); routers globally preferred the more-specific. YouTube responded by advertising *two* `/25`s to reclaim traffic — a Renesys engineer called it *"an absolute hack, in the finest sense of the word."* **Lesson:** BGP censorship is *coarse and leaky* — Pakistan's *intent* was purely domestic; the *effect* was global. "Border in name only." |
| **BGP as a Kill Switch — and Its Defenses** | **Egypt Jan 2011:** Renesys / James Cowie, "Egypt Leaves the Internet" — see [BGP hijacking notable incidents (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BGP_hijacking#Notable_incidents) and Access Now's [KeepItOn tracker](https://www.accessnow.org/keepiton/) for ongoing shutdowns worldwide. **Meta / Facebook Oct 4, 2021 six-hour outage** — BGP withdrawal + intra-DNS dependency + physical-access-lockout: [Facebook engineering post-mortem](https://engineering.fb.com/2021/10/05/networking-traffic/outage-details/) · [Cloudflare's outside analysis](https://blog.cloudflare.com/october-2021-facebook-outage/) — the canonical *"BGP is life support and life kill-switch"* live example. **RPKI:** [RFC 6480 (2012)](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc6480) — prefix owners sign ROAs; routers validate. Adoption stats: [NIST RPKI monitor](https://rpki-monitor.antd.nist.gov/) — roughly 50%+ of announced IPv4 space now covered, but validation-side adoption at ASes is what actually matters and lags. **MANRS:** [manrs.org](https://www.manrs.org/). **Contrast with DNS:** if a DNS resolver lies, you switch resolvers. If BGP withdraws your route, there's *no route* — you can't switch. |
| **Block Pages: Censorship You Can See** | **The measurement classic:** Jones et al., ["Automated Detection and Fingerprinting of Censorship Block Pages" (IMC 2014)](https://conferences.sigcomm.org/imc/2014/papers/p299.pdf) — the fingerprint work behind the slide's table; detects vendor by header/comment signatures. **The commercial vendors named on-slide:** **FortiGuard / Fortinet** ([fortiguard.com](https://www.fortiguard.com/)) — commercial URL-filtering service embedded in Fortinet firewalls. **Netsweeper (Canadian)** — deployed by many state censors; Citizen Lab has documented deployments in Yemen, Bahrain, Kuwait, UAE, and more ([Citizen Lab Netsweeper coverage](https://citizenlab.ca/tag/netsweeper/)). **Websense / Forcepoint** — enterprise-grade URL categorization. **Squid + WireFilter** — open-source, common in smaller-scale ISP filtering. **Detection trick from the book:** block pages are usually *much shorter* than the real page — a length comparison is often enough, no ML needed. **Mechanisms drift.** OONI can plot the shifts on their [Explorer](https://explorer.ooni.org/). |
| **Encryption Is Eroding On-Path Filtering** | **The arc of the deck (and the course) in one slide** — as encryption closes each window, censors move to coarser tools and further up the stack. **SNI filtering — the last cleartext frontier** works because TLS's ClientHello sends the target hostname in the clear for virtual-hosting purposes; Iran, China, Russia all deploy SNI-based filtering. **ECH — the response:** [Cloudflare's ECH announcement (Sep 2023)](https://blog.cloudflare.com/announcing-encrypted-client-hello/). **The blocking-ECH counter-response** — Russia (Aug 2024), China (ongoing). This is a live cat-and-mouse. **Where it goes next:** platforms. Instagram, TikTok, YouTube each control what reaches users at the *application* layer, regardless of what happens at the network layer — the Ch. 3 setup. |
| **Throttling Slows Instead of Blocks** | **The Russia-YouTube case (2024–2025).** Started late July 2024; by Jan 2025 YouTube traffic had collapsed from ~43% → 6–12% of Russian internet, without a formal block. Sources: [OONI report on YouTube in Russia (Nov 2024)](https://ooni.org/post/2024-russia-throttles-youtube/) — SNI-based throttling on `googlevideo.com`, dropping packets to cap video quality; [DFRLab's detailed technical analysis (Jan 2025)](https://dfrlab.org/2025/01/31/how-russia-throttled-youtube-for-domestic-audiences/); [Carnegie Endowment (Feb 2025)](https://carnegieendowment.org/russia-eurasia/politika/2025/02/russia-youtube-block-attempt?lang=en). **Iran throttling of Instagram/WhatsApp** — long-running deniable throttling: [OONI 2022 Iran multi-stakeholder report](https://ooni.org/post/2022-iran-technical-multistakeholder-report/). Roya Ensafi's group at Censored Planet has extensive work on ICMP-rate-limiting and throttling detection. **This slide is the *perfect* embodiment of Roberts's friction concept from Ch. 1** — no block page, no error, just "YouTube is slow lately." Roskomnadzor never had to admit a block; usage collapsed anyway; VPN demand spiked. Throttling is the hardest technique to *reliably* detect (Ch. 5 comes back to this). |
| **Comparing the Techniques** | **Book Table 2.2** — the synthesis. Easier-to-deploy (DNS) also easier to circumvent and detect; heavier hammer (BGP) is hard to aim and has global collateral; throttling trades bluntness for *deniability*. Censors mix techniques because no single technique is cheap, precise, invisible, *and* effective all at once. **Callback to Ch. 1's friction/flooding/fear:** DNS and TCP/RST tend to be *fear* (visible), block pages are *fear*, throttling is *friction* (deniable). BGP is fear at the extreme. |
| **Takeaways** | **The Internet's original trust assumptions are the root cause** — [RFC 3439](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc3439) + Clark's design-philosophy paper are the primary-source anchors. **DNS: easy up, easy down** — the first layer censors reach for and the first layer users circumvent (`8.8.8.8`, `1.1.1.1`, DoH). Encrypted DNS *centralizes* the problem — good for confidentiality against ISPs, bad for concentration risk against the resolver operator. **BGP: rare but devastating** — Egypt 2011, China Telecom 2010, Pakistan/YouTube 2008; RPKI/MANRS are the defenses and adoption still isn't universal. **Encryption keeps pushing the frontier up the stack** — TLS closed content inspection; SNI was next; ECH is closing SNI; blocking ECH is the current front line; the next escalation is platform-layer control (Ch. 3). **Ch. 5 (Measurement) will show *how we know all this*** — Turkey 8.8.8.8, Russia YouTube, and where the evidence gets thinner. |
