| **"The Net Interprets Censorship as Damage"** | Quote is by [John Gilmore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gilmore_(activist)), EFF co-founder. The *Time* piece it appears in: [Philip Elmer-DeWitt, "First Nation in Cyberspace," *Time*, Dec 6, 1993](https://time.com/archive/6724389/first-nation-in-cyberspace/) — the title itself is a David Farber quote about applying to the UN as "the first nation in cyberspace"; the Gilmore "censorship as damage" line is one of several standout quotes. Provenance write-up (with receipts on when Gilmore first said it): [Quote Investigator](https://quoteinvestigator.com/2021/07/12/censor/) — earliest independent record is Rheingold's *The Virtual Community* (Sept 1993), a few months before the *Time* piece. Context: commercial-Internet dawn; AOL/Prodigy/CompuServe just starting to peer with the public Net; founding libertarian optimism about routing around any single point of control. |
| **What This Course Is About** | Course home: <https://noise-lab.github.io/censorship-course/>. Companion book (`censorship-book`) — read Chapter 1 for the framing. This is an electives course built around the companion book: research/reading/discussion heavy, with a technical spine and a final project. Point students at the syllabus. |
| **From "Censorship" to "Information Control"** | The definition on the vignette is straight from Chapter 1 of the book. **Margaret (Molly) Roberts, [*Censored: Distraction and Diversion Inside China's Great Firewall*](https://press.princeton.edu/books/hardcover/9780691178868/censored)** (Princeton, 2018) is the canonical academic treatment — the book that introduces the fear/friction/flooding framework we use all term. Author's page: <https://margaretroberts.net/>. Teaching point: most modern control isn't a firewall that says "BLOCKED" — it's a *tax on access*: make the content slower, harder to find, or buried under noise. Plausible deniability for the censor. |
| **Why Control Information?** | Book §1.1 "The Reasons for Information Control." The point: a throttle is a throttle whether the motive is an election or a moral panic. Separating **mechanism** from **motive** is an analytical move we use all term. |
| **Roberts's Taxonomy: Fear, Friction, Flooding** | [Roberts, *Censored*](https://press.princeton.edu/books/hardcover/9780691178868/censored) — Section 1.2 "Distraction and Diversion" begins on p. 4 and lays out the friction/flooding move. Fear is the old model; friction and flooding are the modern, porous model. "Porous" censorship is effective *because* it has holes — direct repression causes backlash; inconveniencing most people is enough. Alternative frame worth naming if students ask: Jack Balkin's "free speech triangle" (2018, Knight First Amendment Institute essay). |
| **Historical Anchor: The Great Firewall of China** | Overview: [Great Firewall — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Firewall). **Modern GFW targeting TLS ECH / QUIC / DoH:** Hoang et al., *GFWeb: Measuring the Great Firewall's Web Censorship at Scale* (USENIX Security 2024) — search the USENIX Security 2024 accepted-papers page. The book's Chapter 1 refresh cites `gfweb2024`, `gfw2025aicensorship`, and `prc2026publicsecurity` in its bibliography — cross-reference there for exact primary sources. Anchor the "conventional censorship" model here — this is what students think of when they hear the word. Then note the twist: GFW has moved *up the stack* to attack modern anti-censorship protocols themselves. Iran and North Korea have similar (cruder) architectures; China's is the reference. |
| **Democracies Do This Too** | **Egypt Jan 2011 BGP withdrawal (~88% of Egyptian networks disconnected):** the canonical post-mortem is Cowie/Renesys's "Egypt Leaves the Internet" — the book cites `bgpmon2011egypt`. **BART San Francisco, Aug 11, 2011 (cell service shut in four downtown stations):** [EFF public-interest FCC filing (Aug 2011)](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2011/08/public-interest-groups-fcc-bart-cell-phone) · [EFF follow-up (2012)](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2012/05/eff-asks-fcc-forbid-cell-phone-shutdowns-wake-2011-bart-incident) · [ACLU of Northern California retrospective](https://www.aclunc.org/blog/five-years-later-barts-cell-service-shutdown-still-wakeup-call). **Turkey, 2014→:** the "spray-painted `8.8.8.8`" iconography is real; retail-scale ISP DNS blocking is documented at [OONI Explorer](https://explorer.ooni.org/). For the Wikipedia-in-Turkey case study, see [Block of Wikipedia in Turkey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Block_of_Wikipedia_in_Turkey). **Access Now KeepItOn 2025 (313 shutdowns / 52 countries / India-65):** <https://www.accessnow.org/internet-shutdowns-2025/>. **Freedom House Freedom on the Net 2025:** <https://freedomhouse.org/report/freedom-net/2025>. |
| **Case Study: The 2026 Age-Verification Wave** | **UK ICO — Reddit £14.47M fine (2026):** <https://ico-newsroom.prgloo.com/news/ico-issues-reddit-with-gbp-14-47m-fine-for-childrens-privacy-failures>. **UK Ofcom escalating OSA enforcement (4chan, AVS Group, Kick.com, Imgur):** [Inforrm coverage, Mar 2026](https://inforrm.org/2026/03/11/ofcom-steps-up-online-safety-act-enforcement-with-two-further-age-assurance-fines-for-pornographic-platforms-alexandros-antoniou/). **Australia under-16 ban (Dec 10, 2025):** [eSafety Commissioner overview](https://www.esafety.gov.au/about-us/industry-regulation/social-media-age-restrictions) · [NPR](https://www.npr.org/2025/12/10/nx-s1-5639694/social-media-ban-children-australia) · [Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2025/12/9/australias-social-media-ban-for-young-people-takes-effect) · [DLA Piper regulatory guidance overview](https://privacymatters.dlapiper.com/2026/02/australias-social-media-ban-and-the-esafety-commissioners-social-media-minimum-age-regulatory-guidance/). By 16 Jan 2026, eSafety reported ~4.7M under-16 accounts removed across Australia. Penalties up to A$54.6M. **EU DSA age-verification wallet (Apr 2026):** European Commission's [Digital Strategy Age Verification portal](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/). Background note: the ICO fine (data-protection regulator) is often confused with Ofcom fines (Online Safety Act) — separate regimes, coordinating on the same problem. |
| **Case Study: Consolidation as Chokepoint** | **The Nov 18, 2025 Cloudflare outage** (topical anchor) — official post-mortem: [Cloudflare blog](https://blog.cloudflare.com/18-november-2025-outage/). Coverage: [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/11/18/cloudflare-down-outage-traffic-spike-x-chatgpt.html) · [Forbes](https://www.forbes.com/sites/siladityaray/2025/11/18/cloudflare-outage-knocks-x-and-chatgpt-offline/). **The Matthew Prince quote** — date: Aug 16, 2017; days after the [Unite the Right rally in Charlottesville](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unite_the_Right_rally) (Aug 11–12, 2017), Cloudflare terminated *The Daily Stormer*. Primary source (Prince's own writeup): [Why We Terminated Daily Stormer](https://blog.cloudflare.com/why-we-terminated-daily-stormer/). The canonical line: *"Literally, I woke up in a bad mood and decided someone shouldn't be allowed on the Internet. No one should have that power."* Also from the post: *"Someone on our team asked after I announced we were going to terminate Daily Stormer: 'Is this the day the Internet dies?' He was half joking, but only half."* Why the quote sticks: Prince publicly agreeing that Cloudflare *shouldn't* have that power — but exercising it anyway — is the canonical statement of the unelected-arbiter problem. Related follow-ups: Cloudflare terminated 8chan (Aug 2019, after the [El Paso Walmart shooting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019_El_Paso_shooting)) and Kiwi Farms (Sept 2022). We'll return to the whole arc in Lecture 3. |
| **Economic Levers: Paywalls, Demonetization, Zero-Rating** | **Paywall data:** Pew Research 2024 — 74% of Americans encounter paywalls; 1% pay when they hit one; 30% highest-income vs 8% lowest-income pay for news. Main dataset published on <https://www.pewresearch.org/journalism/>. **YouTube "Adpocalypse" (2017):** [Wikipedia summary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adpocalypse). **Caplan & Gillespie, "Tiered Governance and Demonetization,"** *Social Media + Society* (2020) — the canonical academic treatment. **Facebook Free Basics / zero-rating pushback (India TRAI, 2016):** [Internet.org — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet.org) covers the zero-rating controversy. Book §1 and §4.4–4.5. The economic lever is often the strongest — pricing does the work of blocking. |

| **How Control Is Implemented (Technical)** | A preview of Ch. 2–3. Don't go deep here — just establish that there are many points of control between Alice and Bob (access ISPs, transit, CDNs, platforms, endpoint), and the censor can act at any of them. We'll spend weeks on these. |
| **Non-Technical Control Is Just as Real** | Book §1.2 and Ch. 4. The legal/economic lever is often *more* powerful than the technical one — a payment processor or a CA can deplatform faster than a firewall. Legal anchors worth naming: [DMCA §512](https://www.copyright.gov/dmca/) · [CDA §230](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/47/230) · [EU DSA overview (European Commission)](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/digital-services-act-package). Sets up the Legal chapter. |
| **How Widespread Is It?** | Transition slide into the live data. The old slide cited ">60 countries / Freedom on the Net" — we refresh with current shutdown data on the next slide rather than citing a stale number. |
| **A 2026 Vignette: Shutdowns Keep Climbing** | **Primary source (Access Now KeepItOn 2025 report):** <https://www.accessnow.org/internet-shutdowns-2025/> — 313 shutdowns across 52 countries in 2025 (up from 296 in 2024); India leads at 65 events; conflict the leading trigger for the third year running; 70 shutdowns coincided with grave human-rights abuses in 21 countries. Companion measurement resources: [OONI Explorer](https://explorer.ooni.org/) · [Google Transparency Report](https://transparencyreport.google.com/) · [India shutdown tracker (SFLC.in)](https://internetshutdowns.in/). Teaching point: even as control gets subtle (friction/flooding), the crudest tool — full shutdown — is at an all-time high, concentrated in conflict and elections. |
| **We Can Measure It** | Preview of Ch. 5 (Measurement). Crowdsourced and infrastructure measurement — [OONI](https://ooni.org/), Censored Planet ([censoredplanet.org](https://censoredplanet.org/)), Google Transparency Report, shutdown trackers — turn anecdotes into evidence. The whole field exists because control is often designed to look like ordinary failure — measurement is how we tell censorship from a bad day on the network. Hands-on OONI and RouteViews later. |
| **The Trend: From Protocols to Platforms** | **Zuckerberg "squirrel dying" quote:** originally reported in David Kirkpatrick's [*The Facebook Effect*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Facebook_Effect) (Simon & Schuster, 2010). **Eli Pariser's filter bubble:** [Filter bubble — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Filter_bubble); Pariser's book *The Filter Bubble* (Penguin, 2011). Arc of the course and the book: control moved up the stack from pipes to platforms. Centralization is what makes modern takedown both easy and deniable. |
| **Why Bother, If It's Circumventable?** | "Porous censorship" — Roberts, *Censored*, Ch. 1–2. Course central puzzle stated plainly. Resolve it with the "tax" framing: you don't need a perfect wall, just a toll most people won't pay. Motivated few get through (Ch. 6, circumvention) — often acceptable to the censor. VPN / Tor / circumvention preview: Chapter 6. Discussion docs: <https://noise-lab.github.io/censorship-course/breakouts/vpn> and <https://noise-lab.github.io/censorship-course/breakouts/tor>. |
