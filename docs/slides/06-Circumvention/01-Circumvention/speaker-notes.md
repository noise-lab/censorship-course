# Lecture 11 — Speaker Notes (Circumvention: VPNs, Tor, Anonymous Communication)

Per-slide context + clickable links. In-deck notes (the `::: {.notes}` blocks) are also visible via reveal.js speaker view (press **S**). Every URL was verified this session or is a canonical org landing page.

| Slide | Context + Links |
|---|---|
| **The Cat and the Mouse** | The book's recurring metaphor for Ch. 6. Frame every tool as one move in an adversarial game — no permanent win, only a shifting balance of cost. Roberts, [*Censored*](https://press.princeton.edu/books/hardcover/9780691178868/censored) (Princeton, 2018) supplies the "tax on access" vocabulary. |
| **Two Cross-Cutting Themes** | Legality varies (China criminalized unauthorized VPNs in 2017; UAE/Russia/Iran restrict them; US/EU generally legal). Accessibility beats capability — a non-technical user under pressure needs one-click clients. Book Ch. 6 intro. |
| **Why a Proxy at All?** | The Internet is a public network — encryption hides payload, not routing headers. A proxy outside the firewall breaks the passive-observer line of sight. Book §6.1. |
| **What a VPN Actually Does** | Encrypt-and-tunnel to a server on the uncensored Internet. Three implicit assumptions that all can fail: (1) VPN server reachable, (2) provider trustworthy, (3) tunnel unbroken. Enterprises use the same mechanism for remote work — that's where most students first meet a VPN. |
| **VPNs Are Not a Panacea** | **Onavo** — the canonical cautionary tale: Facebook marketed it as a privacy VPN while eavesdropping on the traffic; pulled from app stores 2018, rebranded "Facebook Research," shut down 2020. See [Onavo (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Onavo). Marquee current-event anchor: **hidden Chinese ownership of top consumer VPNs.** The [Tech Transparency Project's April 2025 investigation](https://www.techtransparencyproject.org/articles/apple-offers-apps-with-ties-to-chinese-military) found ~1 in 5 of the top 100 free VPNs in the US App Store were secretly owned by Chinese companies, several traced back to **Qihoo 360** (US-sanctioned "Chinese Military Company"). Follow-up: [TTP spot-check, June 2025](https://www.techtransparencyproject.org/articles/spot-check-apple-and-google-still-have-a-chinese-vpn-problem). Coverage: [Malwarebytes](https://www.malwarebytes.com/blog/news/2025/04/popular-vpns-are-routing-traffic-via-chinese-companies-including-one-with-link-to-military). Provider *consolidation* rhyme: **Kape Technologies** (owner Teddy Sagi) rolled up **CyberGhost (2017), ZenMate (2018), Private Internet Access (2019), ExpressVPN (2021 — $936M)** — see [Kape Technologies (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kape_Technologies). Kape went private in early 2026, removing corporate-filing transparency entirely. |
| **How People Actually Use VPNs** | Our own research: Dutkowska-Żuk, Feamster et al., ["How and Why People Use Virtual Private Networks" (USENIX Security 2022)](https://www.usenix.org/conference/usenixsecurity22/presentation/dutkowska-zuk). The headline: users pick on cost/security/speed; they *believe* free VPNs collect their data and use them anyway. Trust in institutional VPNs > commercial. |
| **Censorship Can *Increase* Access** | Roberts's paradox from *Censored*: censorship events permanently shift behavior toward circumvention tools. Nigeria 2021 Twitter ban: see [Wikipedia — 2021 Twitter blockage in Nigeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2021_Twitter_blockage_in_Nigeria). Pakistan 2024–25 X restrictions (HTTPS-layer interference) — see [OONI Pakistan](https://explorer.ooni.org/country/PK). Freshest example — Uganda 2026: during the January election shutdown, Proton VPN sign-ups in Uganda spiked **~3,000% over baseline** ([Tom's Guide, Jan 2026](https://www.tomsguide.com/computing/vpns/vpn-usage-soars-in-uganda-as-internet-access-returns-but-privacy-tools-may-be-under-attack)). |
| **The Cat-and-Mouse Game, Concretely** | **Marquee current-event data.** By mid-January 2026, [Roskomnadzor had restricted access to 439 VPN services](https://tadviser.com/index.php/Article:Roskomnadzor%60s_policy_on_Internet_control) — 70% more than October 2025 — with a stated target of **92% VPN blocking effectiveness by 2030**, funded at ~20 billion rubles/year via the ASBI/TSPU program. See [TechRadar (Feb 2025)](https://www.techradar.com/vpn/vpn-privacy-security/russias-censor-body-roskomnadzor-wants-to-block-92-percent-of-vpn-apps-by-2030-and-its-investing-20-billion-rubles-a-year-to-build-a-permanent-vpn-censorship-system) and [TechRadar's 2026 outlook](https://www.techradar.com/vpn/vpn-services/russias-battle-against-vpns-is-entering-a-new-phase-heres-what-to-expect-in-2026). **Decree No. 1667** (Oct 2025) elevated Roskomnadzor to a "super-regulator" that can block directly via DPI without ISP cooperation. Yet Russians still downloaded VPNs **9+ million times in March 2026 alone** — see [Carnegie Endowment, May 2026 — "Kremlin Struggles to Solve a VPN Problem of Its Own Making"](https://carnegieendowment.org/russia-eurasia/politika/2026/05/russia-vpn-usage-political). |
| **Chaum's Mix: The Building Block** | The conceptual ancestor of onion routing. Chaum, ["Untraceable Electronic Mail, Return Addresses, and Digital Pseudonyms" (CACM 1981)](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/358549.358563). Anonymity from *batching + delay* — fine for email, terrible for the web. |
| **Tor: Low-Latency Onion Routing** | Second-generation onion routing, 20+ years old. Landing: [torproject.org](https://www.torproject.org/). Live stats: [Tor Metrics](https://metrics.torproject.org/). Foundational paper: Dingledine, Mathewson & Syverson, ["Tor: The Second-Generation Onion Router" (USENIX Security 2004)](https://svn.torproject.org/svn/projects/design-paper/tor-design.pdf). The design move that makes Tor usable: pairwise symmetric keys established during circuit construction, not per-packet asymmetric ops. |
| **Tor's Threat Model and Its Limits** | Safe unless adversary sees both entry and exit. Cracks: end-to-end correlation, [website fingerprinting](https://www.usenix.org/conference/usenixsecurity16/technical-sessions/presentation/panchenko), DNS leaks, rogue exits, browser fingerprinting, user login mistakes. "Tor isn't broken" applies to the crypto, not the user. |
| **Onion Services and the "Dark Web"** | Legit onion mirrors exist: [BBC .onion](https://www.bbcnewsd73hkzno2ini43t4gblxvycyac5aw4gnv7t2rccijh7745uqd.onion/) (announcement: [BBC blog, 2019](https://www.bbc.com/news/technology-50150981)); [NYT .onion](https://www.nytimes3xbfgragh.onion/) (announcement: [NYT Open, 2017](https://open.nytimes.com/https-open-nytimes-com-the-new-york-times-as-a-tor-onion-service-e0d0b67b7482)); [ProPublica .onion](https://www.propub3r6espa33w.onion/). Dual-use anchor: **Ross Ulbricht / Silk Road** — Trump pardoned Ulbricht on **January 21, 2025** ([NPR](https://www.npr.org/2025/01/21/nx-s1-5270051/trump-pardons-dark-web-marketplace-creator-ross-ulbricht) · [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/01/21/trump-pardons-silk-road-ross-ulbricht-.html) · [Ross Ulbricht (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ross_Ulbricht)). Ulbricht had been serving two life sentences; the pardon reignited the anonymity-vs-abuse policy debate. Structural legal risk to Tor operators: German police raid on **Artikel 5 e.V. (Essen)** exit-node operator on **August 16, 2024** — see [Cybernews report](https://cybernews.com/privacy/tor-exit-node-operator-raided-germany/) and [Tor Project forum thread](https://forum.torproject.org/t/tor-relays-artikel-5-e-v-another-police-raid-in-germany-general-assembly-on-sep-21st-2024/14533). The still-controlling precedent for exit-node operators is the 2014 Graz case (Austria). |
| **Beyond Tor: Pick a Portfolio** | Landing pages: [Psiphon](https://psiphon.ca/), [Lantern](https://lantern.io/), [I2P](https://geti2p.net/). Practical takeaway: a journalist might use Tor+obfs4 for source contact, a VPN for routine work, and Psiphon as a shutdown fallback. Psiphon absorbed huge surges during Myanmar's 2021 coup and Iran's 2022–23 protests. |
| **Pluggable Transports: Disguise the Traffic** | Overview: [Tor Project — Pluggable Transports](https://tb-manual.torproject.org/circumvention/). Randomization family — **obfs4** ([spec](https://gitlab.com/yawning/obfs4)); mimicry family — **meek** and **WebTunnel** ([Tor blog announcement, March 2024](https://blog.torproject.org/introducing-webtunnel-evading-censorship-by-hiding-in-plain-sight/)). **Marquee current event:** by mid-2025, Roskomnadzor had enumerated and blocked many WebTunnel bridges by domain — see [Tor Project — call for more WebTunnel bridges (Nov 2024)](https://blog.torproject.org/call-for-webtunnel-bridges/) and [CyberInsider coverage](https://cyberinsider.com/tor-issues-urgent-call-for-webtunnel-bridge-operators-to-curb-blocks/). Tor's response: distribute bridges over Telegram (harder to enumerate) and steer operators away from Hetzner/OVH/DigitalOcean/Cloudflare DNS. The cat-and-mouse game *inside* the cat-and-mouse game. |
| **Domain Fronting** | Foundational: Fifield et al., ["Blocking-resistant communication through domain fronting" (PETS 2015)](https://www.bamsoftware.com/papers/fronting/). Kill-off story: [Signal — "A letter from Amazon" (May 1, 2018)](https://signal.org/blog/looking-back-on-the-front/) is required reading. Timeline: Google App Engine killed fronting April 14 2018; AWS CloudFront followed April 27 2018; Microsoft in 2022. The domino began earlier — Cloudflare blocked the technique in 2015. Coverage: [CyberScoop](https://cyberscoop.com/domain-fronting-future-amazon-google-microsoft-cloudflare-tor-signal/). Since 2022 Iran-protest lobbying to reinstate for humanitarian use has gone nowhere: [MIT Tech Review, Nov 2022](https://www.technologyreview.com/2022/11/11/1063107/big-tech-iran-protests-domain-fronting/). |
| **Steganography: Hide the Channel Itself** | Goal: hide the *existence* of communication, not just contents. Deniability can be deterministic or statistical. Image steganography here is illustrative — practical circumvention systems have mostly moved on. |
| **Covert Channels: Infranet and Collage** | **Infranet** — Feamster et al., ["Infranet: Circumventing Web Censorship and Surveillance" (USENIX Security 2002)](https://www.usenix.org/legacy/event/sec02/full_papers/feamster/feamster.pdf). **Collage** — Burnett, Feamster & Vempala, ["Chipping Away at Censorship Firewalls with User-Generated Content" (USENIX Security 2010)](https://www.usenix.org/legacy/event/sec10/tech/full_papers/Burnett.pdf). Arc: from your own infrastructure → piggybacking on UGC hosts that already serve billions of images. Task mapping hashes a message ID to "tasks" like "search blue flowers on Flickr." |
| **Why Information Hiding Stays Niche** | "The Parrot Is Dead" — Houmansadr, Brubaker & Shmatikov, ["The Parrot is Dead: Observing Unobservable Network Communications" (IEEE S&P 2013)](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/6547106) ([paper](https://people.cs.umass.edu/~amir/papers/parrot.pdf)). Key lesson: faithfully imitating a real protocol is harder than *implementing* it — every quirk is a fingerprint. Practical niche today: **bootstrapping** — sneak a bridge address past the censor, then switch to a faster tool. |
| **Decoy Routing** | Three near-simultaneous 2011 proposals: **Curveball** — Karlin et al., ["Decoy Routing: Toward Unblockable Internet Communication" (FOCI 2011)](https://www.usenix.org/legacy/events/foci11/tech/final_files/Karlin.pdf); **Telex** — Wustrow et al., ["Telex: Anticensorship in the Network Infrastructure" (USENIX Security 2011)](https://www.usenix.org/conference/usenix-security-11/telex-anticensorship-network-infrastructure); **Cirripede** — Houmansadr et al., ["Cirripede: Circumvention Infrastructure using Router Redirection" (CCS 2011)](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/2046707.2046730). Sentinel + on-path cooperating router — no proxy endpoint for the censor to enumerate. |
| **Decoy Routing: Promise vs. Deployment** | Technically the most elegant idea in the chapter and the least deployed — the blocker is *incentives*, not crypto. An ISP gains nothing by helping users abroad. As software routers get faster, on-path middlebox deployment may become feasible; the deployment story hasn't materialized yet. |
| **The Whole Chapter in One Picture** | Synthesis: read up the stack of what you're hiding — payload → identity → the fact of circumvention → the channel → the endpoint. Every layer raises the censor's cost; none is a final answer. Effective practice = a portfolio matched to a threat model. |
| **Taking Back Control** | Close on Ch. 6's two cross-cutting themes: legality varies, and the most usable tool usually beats the most capable one. Next class: Ch. 7, the Conclusion / Internet governance. |
